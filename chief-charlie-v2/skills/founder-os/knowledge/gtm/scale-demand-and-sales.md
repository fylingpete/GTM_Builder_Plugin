# Make growth scalable

Phase 2.3 is where most B2B startups die. The motion works, two reps
hit quota, and the founder thinks: *time to hit the gas.* They hire 10
reps and double the ad budget. Six months later, reps sit idle, the
single channel has saturated, and bookings are flat despite headcount
doubling.

Skok's diagnosis: **Phase 2.3 is not "more of the same, just bigger."
It is a new business motion** — Demand-Gen layered on top of Sales,
operating as a system of overlapping S-curves. Get this wrong and 2.5
(profitability) is unreachable.

## The bookings formula

Skok's framework, paraphrased:

```
Bookings = Number of Reps × Productivity-per-Rep (PPR)
```

To grow bookings you have exactly two levers: more reps, or each rep
sells more. Both have constraints:

- **More reps** require more leads. If demand-gen plateaus, PPR drops.
- **Higher PPR** requires either better leads, better playbook, or
  better reps. All three take quarters to move.

**Growth comes in discontinuous units** as new reps ramp (typically
3-6 months). A rep hired in January doesn't contribute fully until
April-June. Plan accordingly.

## The lead-supply trap (#1 cause of 2.3 failure)

Every individual lead source has a saturation point and an S-curve:

- **Paid search** exhausts the volume of buyers searching for your
  category. You can outbid competitors, but the audience is finite.
- **Paid social** exhausts the targetable audience. CTR drops, CPC
  rises.
- **Outbound** exhausts the addressable list at any given response
  rate.
- **Content/SEO** has compounding returns but slow ramp (12+ months).

A scaling B2B startup must **layer multiple sources**, so that as one
approaches its ceiling, another is ramping up:

- Quarter 1-4: Paid search dominates
- Quarter 4-8: Paid social adds on top
- Quarter 8-12: Content / SEO / events / outbound layer in

Total lead volume keeps rising even as each individual source hits
its limit. **The job of the demand-gen function is constantly to be
experimenting with the next source to add before the current ones
plateau.**

## The first marketing hire

The right first marketing hire **knows how to generate leads** — often
called Demand Gen. Forget branding, marcom, PR for now; those are
secondary. **Branding doesn't fill a pipeline.**

**Interview signal:** ask for a track record of campaigns they
personally ran with measurable lead output. Vague references to
"strategy" or "brand work" are red flags. Great marketing at this
stage is execution. Ideas are cheap; the question is whether the
candidate has shipped lead-generating campaigns.

**Reporting line:** the demand-gen lead reports to the CEO until ~$5M
ARR (Schatz), then to a VP Marketing.

## The marketing-sales contract (cohort-based)

Skok's funnel insight: **funnel conversion rates require cohort
analysis, not snapshots.** Snapshot conversion lies because each lead
takes weeks to months to traverse the funnel.

Track each January cohort across 1, 2, 3, 6 months to see real
conversion. Reverse-engineer from desired closes:

```
1000 closes/quarter (target)
  ÷ 25% close rate from SQL
= 4000 SQLs needed
  ÷ 30% qualification rate from MQL
= 13,333 MQLs needed
  ÷ 5% conversion from raw lead
= 266,000 leads needed
```

This is the **marketing-sales contract**: marketing commits to
266K leads/quarter at the agreed quality bar, sales commits to the
conversion rates. When numbers miss, the side that owns the gap fixes it.

Without this contract, sales blames marketing for "bad leads" and
marketing blames sales for "not following up." Both are right and
neither has a fixable problem.

## Sales-team scaling — the pod

Skok and Kazanjy's "revenue mitochondria": clone tight pods, not
isolated reps.

A pod is:
- 1 SDR (lead qualification)
- 2-3 AEs (closing)
- 1 CSM (post-sale, owns retention)

When you scale, you add pods, not isolated AEs. A solo AE without an
SDR pipeline runs out of qualified leads; a solo AE without a CSM
hands deals into a black hole.

**Pod-cloning rule:** before adding pod #2, pod #1 must be hitting
quota with stable PPR for 2 quarters.

## Sales operations — the system layer

This is what most founders miss. Hormozi's operational frameworks:

- **Lead routing** (60-second speed-to-contact = 4× revenue vs delayed)
- **Sales specialization** (split SDR/AE/CSM — specialization shortens
  training time and increases close rates)
- **On-call / Off-call SOPs** (top reps have two SOPs — what they do
  on calls AND what they do between calls)
- **Maximize availability** (the single greatest lever on sales
  throughput is total available time slots — availability beats talent)
- **Pull up appointments** (today's appointments have higher show
  and close rates than future appointments — pull them up)

Plus the system tooling: CRM with required fields enforced, pipeline
stages clearly defined, reporting cadence (daily dash for energy per
Schatz, weekly pipeline review, monthly bookings review).

## Pipeline forecasting (Schatz)

Schatz's discipline: every Monday morning, every rep updates their
pipeline with stage, expected close date, deal size, win confidence.
Pipeline health is the leading indicator of bookings 60-90 days out.

Two metrics to watch:
- **Pipeline coverage** = pipeline value ÷ quota. Target: 3-4×.
- **Pipeline velocity** = (deals × win rate × deal size) ÷ cycle time.
  Track week-over-week.

When pipeline coverage drops below 3×, it's a leading indicator of
a bookings miss in 60-90 days. Marketing must add leads or sales
must compress cycle time.

## Bookings vs ARR — Skok's leading-indicator rule

**Bookings = Net New ARR = New + Expansion - Churned.**

ARR is a cumulative sum and lags reality by quarters. **Flat bookings
produce a slowly rising ARR line that hides a stalled business.**

Track bookings monthly, ARR quarterly. If bookings flatlines, you
have ~6 months before ARR shows it — and ~12 months before
investors see it. Catch it in bookings.

## Exit criteria

Phase 2.3 is done when:

1. ≥2 lead sources contribute >20% each to total leads (no
   single-channel dependence)
2. Bookings plan is on paper and the lead-supply plan supports it
   without saturation assumptions
3. ≥2 sales pods at quota for ≥2 quarters
4. CRM hygiene: 100% of opportunities have stage, close date, and
   deal size; pipeline review is weekly with documented decisions
5. Demand-gen lead is hired and shipping campaigns

## Anti-patterns

- **Scaling sales headcount without scaling lead supply.** Reps go
  idle, PPR drops, you blame the reps.
- **One-channel dependence.** Paid search alone cannot scale you to
  $50M. Add the next channel BEFORE the first one saturates.
- **First marketing hire is "head of brand."** Wrong profile.
  Branding doesn't fill pipeline. Hire demand-gen.
- **No marketing-sales contract.** Both sides argue about lead
  quality forever; neither side has accountability.
- **Skipping pod structure.** Solo AEs don't scale. Pods scale.
- **ARR-watching, ignoring bookings.** ARR rises while bookings
  stall = you have ~2 quarters before everyone notices.

## Connection to other steps

- **From 2.2:** The two-rep cohort is your first pod. Now you clone it.
- **From 2.1:** The single channel from 2.1 is now lead source #1
  of N. You add #2, then #3.
- **To 2.4 (Customers succeed):** Scaling sales WITHOUT fixing
  retention is what causes the Skok Phase 7 churn spike. Watch
  cohort retention as you scale.
- **To 2.5 (Make it profitable):** Channel CAC per source feeds
  directly into the LTV:CAC analysis. If a new channel has 3× the
  CAC of the original, it may break unit economics.
