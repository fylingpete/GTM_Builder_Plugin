## Tactic: Founder-Led Sales Process — Y Combinator

Founders should learn to sell before hiring anyone. You can't outsource engineering, and you shouldn't outsource sales either — it has to be part of the founders' DNA. If you know the problem, the product, and the market, you're already an expert in the eyes of the customer.

### Writing the Sales Email

Keep it to 6-8 sentences max. Plain text, no HTML formatting, no jargon:
- Say exactly what you do and how it works
- Address the problem the customer has
- Say you're the founder (people respond to founders)
- Include social proof: YC batch, impressive past companies, relevant expertise
- Link to a simple website with screenshots and bullets — not fancy graphics
- End with a clear call to action (book a call, try it, reply)

### The Sales Funnel (Founder Speak)

1. **Make the list** — Use Google Sheets or a simple CRM. Track industry, company, name, email, LinkedIn
2. **Send emails/LinkedIn messages** — Hundreds, not dozens
3. **Run demos** — Founders do the demos. You know the product and pain points best
4. **Talk pricing and close** — Don't avoid the money conversation
5. **Onboard them** — If you skip this, they churn because your early product isn't self-serve yet

### Work Backwards From Your Goal

Example: You want 2 customers. Send 500 emails → 250 opens (50%) → 12 responses (5%) → 6 demos (50%) → 2 customers (33%). Most founders send 100 emails, get zero customers, and conclude "sales doesn't work." Wrong — you just sent too few emails. Track your conversion rates. Without data, you can't improve.

### Who to Sell To First

- **Easiest customers first** — not the hardest
- **People you know** beat strangers
- **Startups** beat enterprises — shorter decision cycles, no procurement department
- **Early adopters only** — most people archive your email. That's fine. Just send more

## Key Takeaway
Sales is a numbers game with a learnable process — founders must do it themselves first, track every conversion step, and only hire sales people after they know what "good" looks like.

## When to Apply
- You have a product but zero paying customers
- You're considering hiring a salesperson before you've closed deals yourself
- You've tried some outreach but concluded "sales doesn't work for us"
