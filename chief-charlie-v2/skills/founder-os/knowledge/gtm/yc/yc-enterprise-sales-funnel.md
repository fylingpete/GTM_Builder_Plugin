## Framework: The Enterprise Sales Funnel — Y Combinator

A step-by-step process from finding prospects to ensuring they actually use your product.

### 1. Prospecting
Start with a sales hypothesis: "Customer X has problem Y and our product solves it." Use industry lists and tools to find companies matching your hypothesis. Find the specific humans who make buying decisions, not just anyone willing to talk.

### 2. Outreach
Inbound is easier than outbound — launch early, create content, build self-serve demos, answer questions in forums. For cold outreach, seek warm intros first. If your customers attend conferences, be there with pre-scheduled meetings.

### 3. Qualification (First Call)
Don't pitch. Ask questions: What made you take this call? How long have you had this problem? How do you quantify the impact? Who makes the buying decision? What's your budget? If they don't have the problem, budget, or authority — move on. You just saved yourself weeks.

### 4. Demo
Not a feature tour. Tell a story: who is the main character (their user), what's their problem, and watch how your product solves it. Personalize everything — use their logo, their website, their team members' names. Create magic moments where you surprise them with how easy something is. At Optimizely, demoing directly on the customer's website made marketers' eyes light up.

### 5. Pricing
Ask questions before quoting: How much does this problem cost you? What's your budget? How much do you spend on competitors? Then pick a number that makes you slightly uncomfortable. Higher prices filter for serious customers and help validate demand.

### 6. Closing
Ask upfront how they buy software and who needs to sign off. Execute security questionnaires and legal reviews in parallel. Keep contracts simple. Your champion is your biggest ally — stay in constant communication.

### 7. Implementation
The single biggest mistake: thinking implementation is the customer's job. One company signed six-figure deals only to discover a year later customers hadn't used the product once. Treat implementation like a high-priority internal project with a shared roadmap.

## Key Takeaway
Your sales funnel only ends when the customer is using your product habitually — everything before that is just steps toward real value delivery.

## When to Apply
- You're doing enterprise sales for the first time and need a structured process
- Your demos feel like feature tours instead of compelling stories
- You're signing contracts but customers aren't actually implementing
