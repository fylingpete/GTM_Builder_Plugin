## Framework: Identifying Your Best First Customer — Y Combinator

Choosing the wrong first customer can trap you on a path that wastes months. Before building, use user interviews to extract three numerical answers that reveal who to sell to first.

### The 3-Question Venn Diagram

For each potential customer, get hard numbers on:

1. **How much does this problem cost them today?** — Revenue they'd gain or expenses they'd save. Get a dollar number, not a vague "it's painful"
2. **How frequently do they encounter the problem?** — Hourly, daily, weekly, quarterly? Higher frequency means more pain and more chances to test whether your product works
3. **How large is their budget to solve it?** — The person feeling the pain may not control the budget. Make sure you're talking to someone with authority to buy

### The Stack Rank

Put answers in a spreadsheet. The best first customer sits at the center of all three circles — highest cost, highest frequency, highest budget.

### Example: Smart Blender Startup

| Customer | Cost/Opportunity | Frequency | Budget |
|----------|-----------------|-----------|--------|
| French Laundry | High per sale | Low traffic | Low (sous chef, no authority) |
| Google Cafe | Low (free food) | High volume | Low (no revenue motivation) |
| McDonald's | Moderate per sale | Massive volume | Huge (chief food officer controls billions) |

McDonald's wins — not because each smoothie is expensive, but because volume x frequency x budget authority makes it the obvious first target.

### Why This Matters

If potential customers aren't already exploring solutions, the problem may not burn enough. The fourth user interview question — "What have you done to try to solve this?" — tests this. No current attempts = weak demand signal.

## Key Takeaway
Don't sell to whoever responds first — stack-rank prospects by problem cost, frequency, and budget authority, then go after the highest-scoring ones.

## When to Apply
- You have multiple potential customer segments and don't know which to pursue first
- You're spending months on a prospect who can't actually buy
- Early sales feel random rather than strategic
