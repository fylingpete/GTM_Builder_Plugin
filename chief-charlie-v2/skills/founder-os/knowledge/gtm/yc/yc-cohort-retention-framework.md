## Framework: Cohort Retention — Measuring If You've Made Something People Want — Y Combinator

Cohort retention is the single best quantitative way to answer the question: "Have we made something people want?" It tracks what fraction of new users keep using your product over time.

### How to Measure It
Define three things:
1. **Cohorts**: Group users by when they first used your product (usually by week or month)
2. **Action**: What counts as "active" — not just opening the app, but a meaningful action correlated with real value (e.g., viewing 3+ posts on Instagram, completing a ride on Uber)
3. **Time period**: Match your product's intended usage frequency — daily for social apps, weekly for utilities, quarterly for travel apps

### The Only Thing That Matters: Flatness
Compare two products: Product A retains 75% at week 3 but keeps declining. Product B drops to 20% fast but then goes completely flat. Product B is the winner. If curves don't flatten, you're on a treadmill — acquiring new users while losing old ones forever.

A product that retains just 20% permanently will eventually reach massive scale. Google Photos retained ~20-40% of users weekly — within six weeks of launch, it was clear it would eventually reach a billion users.

### How You Fool Yourself
- **Too large a time period**: Switching from weekly to quarterly makes curves look flatter artificially
- **Too easy an action**: Counting "saw a notification bell" as active usage (the Google Plus mistake)
- **Single data points**: "We have 80% week-3 retention" means nothing without the full curve shape
- **Trusting analytics tools blindly**: Build your own curves in a spreadsheet first to develop intuition

## Key Takeaway
If your cohort curves don't get flat, you haven't yet made something people want — get out there and talk to your customers.

## When to Apply
- You're growing users but aren't sure if they're sticking around
- An investor asks about your retention and you don't have a clear answer
- You're reporting vanity metrics instead of measuring real product-market fit
