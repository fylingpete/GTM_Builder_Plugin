## Framework: B2B Pricing — The Value Equation — Y Combinator

Most founders freeze when asked for pricing because they think of consumer prices ($19/month). Enterprise pricing works completely differently — it's based on value delivered, not cost incurred.

### Step 1: Build the Value Equation
Sit with your champion and write down the value your product delivers: cost savings, time savings, or revenue increase. Example: your AI customer service tool eliminates 20% of queries for a team of 100 agents at $100K fully-loaded cost each = $2M potential savings.

### Step 2: Price at One-Third of Value
Charge 25-50% of the value delivered. Customer keeps ~two-thirds, you keep ~one-third. In the example: charge ~$700K for $2M of savings. Your champion can take this ROI calculation to their CFO.

### Step 3: Check Against Cost
Never start with cost-plus pricing — it always leads to underpricing. But verify your costs are well below your price. Aim for 80-90% software gross margins. Treat free credits (AWS, OpenAI) as real costs.

### Step 4: Consider Competition
Don't enter price wars — competing on price alone is a race to the bottom (airlines average 2.7% net margins). Differentiate on functionality and value instead.

### Pricing Structure Tactics
- Mirror how your customers already pay for similar software
- Prefer committed recurring revenue (MRR/ARR) over pure usage-based pricing
- Start usage-based, then convert to minimum monthly commitments with volume discounts
- Ask your champion what amount they can personally approve without CFO sign-off
- Gate enterprise features (SSO, audit logs, SOC 2) behind higher-tier pricing

### If All Else Fails
Pick a number, increase by 50% for each new customer until you lose more than 25% of deals on price alone. Your first customers are a tiny fraction of future revenue — don't over-optimize.

## Key Takeaway
Price on customer value, not your costs — and if every deal closes instantly, you're almost certainly underpricing.

## When to Apply
- A customer asked for pricing and you froze
- You're charging consumer-level prices ($49/month) for enterprise software
- You're losing margin in a price war with competitors
