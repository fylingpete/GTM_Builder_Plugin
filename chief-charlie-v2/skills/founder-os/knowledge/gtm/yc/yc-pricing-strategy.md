## Framework: Five Pricing Insights from Top YC Companies — Y Combinator

Pricing is a learning tool, not a permanent decision. Most founders charge too little or nothing at all.

### 1. You Should Charge
The most common mistake is not charging. Charging teaches you: who's willing to pay, which segments want it most, how much value they see, and whether you've built enough value. Even universal refusal is valuable — it means you haven't built enough yet. Stripe deliberately charged 5% per transaction (nearly double competitors) to test if their developer experience was worth a premium.

### 2. Price on Value, Not Cost
Three components: cost (what it costs you to serve), price (what you charge), value (what customers perceive). The gap between price and value is your opportunity to raise prices. Cost-plus pricing always leads to underpricing. Ask users: does this help you make more money, reduce costs, move faster, or avoid risk?

### 3. You're Almost Certainly Undercharging
The ideal price: customers complain but still pay. If every deal closes instantly, you're too cheap. Higher prices signal quality, create higher margins, and let you outspend competitors on acquisition. Raising prices is the easiest revenue growth lever — changing one number doubles revenue vs. finding thousands of new customers.

### 4. Pricing Isn't Permanent
Exclude existing customers from increases, or give advance notice. Netflix has raised prices repeatedly to 221 million subscribers. If Netflix can do it, you can do it with your handful of early customers.

### 5. Keep It Simple
Complex pricing pages with 5 tiers and crossed-out prices kill conversion. GitLab's three clean plans with clear pricing is the model.

### The Segment Story
Started free → sheepishly charged $120/year → customers said "charge us more, otherwise we worry about keeping data with you" → sales advisor pushed for $120K/year → CEO nervously asked for $120K → customer countered at $12K → settled at $18K. A 150x increase from simply asking.

## Key Takeaway
Treat pricing as an experiment, not a commitment — charge early, raise often, and remember that low prices signal low value.

## When to Apply
- You're giving your product away for free to avoid rejection
- Every customer says yes immediately to your price
- You're afraid that raising prices will lose all your customers
