## Framework: Sales Specialization — Alex Hormozi

When a company has multiple products, lead sources, and customer segments, expecting every salesperson to master everything is unrealistic. Specialization is the answer.

### Four Ways to Specialize

**By product** — Each rep becomes an expert on one product line. They know every objection, every use case, every competitor. Depth beats breadth.

**By lead type** — Inbound leads require different skills than outbound prospects. Warm referrals convert differently than cold outreach responses. Match the rep's strengths to the lead source.

**By territory** — Geographic or industry-based territories let reps develop deep knowledge of their market. They learn the local competitors, pricing norms, and buying patterns.

**By process stage** — Split setters (who qualify and book) from closers (who present and close). Each role requires different skills and temperament. A great qualifier is often a mediocre closer and vice versa.

### The Training Benefit

Specialization dramatically shortens training time. Training someone to master one product takes weeks. Training them on five products takes months — and they never reach the same depth. Shorter training means faster ramp-up, lower training cost, and quicker ROI on new hires.

### Customer Service Parallel

Apply the same principle to support. Triage customer issues to specialized CS reps by product tier. Build career progression across tiers — a junior rep handles tier 1, progresses to tier 2, then tier 3. This provides growth paths that reduce turnover while building deeper expertise.

### Different Compensation

Each specialization may warrant different comp structures. A high-ticket closer gets different incentives than a high-volume setter. Align compensation with the behaviors each role requires.

## Key Takeaway
Stop asking salespeople to sell everything — specialize by product, lead type, territory, or process stage to shorten training and increase close rates.

## When to Apply
- Reps are overwhelmed by too many products or lead sources
- Training new hires takes too long and results are inconsistent
- Close rates are declining as the company adds complexity
