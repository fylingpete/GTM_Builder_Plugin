## Tactic: Pre-Call Preparation and Note-Taking — Alex Hormozi

You'd be amazed at how much of a genius you sound like with five minutes of research. The best salespeople never show up blind.

### The 10% Prep Rule

For recurring calls (daily sales), invest about 10% of the call time in preparation. A 60-minute call gets 6 minutes of prep. A 2-hour meeting gets 12 minutes. For rare, high-stakes presentations, the ratio reverses: 6 hours of prep for 1 hour of presentation.

### What Research Does

When you reference specific details about a prospect — their business, their social profiles, recent news — it triggers know, like, and trust simultaneously. They feel *known* because you took the time. They *like* people who know about them. They *trust* people who demonstrate familiarity, because it approximates an existing relationship. All of this happens in the first 60 seconds instead of wasting the first five minutes proving you're unprepared.

### Coming In With Solutions

Early in selling gym turnaround services, building a profile from scattered social data was the only option since no reputation existed yet. But showing up with "I looked at your floor layout — if you move this here, you could double usable square footage" transformed the dynamic. Instead of asking questions to figure things out on the call, come with problems identified and solutions prepared. Small operators win through preparation; established players win through reputation.

### Take Notes for the Next Call

Public preparation uses what you can find online. Second-call preparation uses what you learned on the phone. Reference their daughter's name, their business situation, the specific challenge they mentioned. They'll think: "I don't have to re-explain my life story. This person's recommendations will fit my context." That's the shift from salesperson to consultant.

## Key Takeaway
Five minutes of pre-call research builds more trust than thirty minutes of small talk — and taking notes turns every follow-up into a consultative conversation.

## When to Apply
- You're new in a market and don't have brand recognition yet
- Prospects feel like they're getting a generic pitch rather than personalized advice
- Follow-up calls start from scratch because no one took notes on the first one
