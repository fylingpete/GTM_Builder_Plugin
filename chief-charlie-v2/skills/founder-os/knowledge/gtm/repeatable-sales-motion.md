# Find a repeatable sales motion

Phase 2.1 starts after PMF (Sean Ellis ≥ 40%). At the start the sales path
is a forest with no trail. By the end it is a written playbook that a
non-founder rep could execute. Skok is explicit: this is the single most
expensive phase to skip — turning up sales spend before the playbook is
written burns 6-9 months and the rep almost always fails.

The output of 2.1 is **one document**, not five separate documents. It
lists, on one page or thereabouts:

- **One target market** — the segment to focus on first
- **One ICP** with positive AND negative characteristics
- **One use case** — the specific pain and benefit
- **One core message** — benefit-led, simple, short, differentiated
- **One channel** — the top-of-funnel that fills the pipeline
- **One sales motion** — how the deal moves stage to stage
- **One price point**

If any of these is "it depends" or "we serve multiple", 2.1 is not done.

## The five mandatory dimensions to lock

### 1. Target Market & ICP — pick exactly one

The HubSpot trap (Brian Halligan, quoted by Skok): *"We argued for years
and by not deciding, every team had to serve multiple personas."* Picking
one target market is what makes every later team's job dramatically easier.

**ICP has two halves**, both in writing:

- **Positive characteristics** — industry, company size, job function,
  use case, key signals (e.g. "uses Salesforce", "raised Series B in
  last 18 months", "has a sales-led motion").
- **Negative characteristics** — the disqualifiers. Sub-50-employee
  companies, heavily regulated industries, customers without an existing
  CRM, founders who want unlimited customization. The negative list is
  what stops sales from wasting cycles on bad-fit deals — and it is what
  prevents churn pain in 2.4.

Selection method (Skok): score candidate segments on three numbers — TAM,
urgency/willingness/ability to pay, and ease of fulfillment. Pick the
highest combined score, not the largest TAM.

**Hair-on-fire urgency is the gating filter.** Without urgency, the
conversations are pleasant but the deals never close — long cycles and
few wins. If the buyer's problem is not on fire, the sales motion will
not be repeatable no matter how good the script is.

### 2. Offer — what they actually get

This is where the ICP meets a concrete value exchange. Hormozi's Grand
Slam Offer formula (paraphrased): make an offer so good people feel
stupid saying no. Components: dream outcome, perceived likelihood of
achievement, time delay, effort and sacrifice. A weak offer with a
strong sales script will not sell; a strong offer with a weak script
often will.

The offer must be specific to the ICP and use case. Generic offers lose
to use-case-specific offers — Skok's example: "automated insights from
your data" loses to "automatically warns you when funnel indicators show
a problem so you can fix issues before bookings drop." Same product,
dramatically different conversion.

### 3. Messaging — five rules

Two artifacts come out of this phase: the ICP and the core messaging.
Both must be in writing. Five rules for the messaging:

- **Business benefits, not product features** (Theodore Levitt:
  *"People don't want to buy a quarter-inch drill. They want a
  quarter-inch hole."*)
- **Clear** — anyone in the buyer persona understands instantly
- **Simple** — no jargon, no internal vocabulary
- **Short** — fits on a single slide or homepage hero
- **Explains differentiation** — why this rather than the alternative

The messaging should describe *the hole the buyer needs to drill in
their business* — the outcome they're paying for — not the features of
your drill.

### 4. Channel — pick exactly ONE to test first

This is the discipline most founders break. Hormozi's Core Four are
**Warm Outreach / Content / Cold Outreach / Paid Ads**. The temptation
is to "try a bit of everything." Skok and Hormozi both forbid it. Pick
one. Run it until you have evidence (positive or negative) before adding
a second.

**Hormozi's recommended order for new founders:**

1. **Warm Outreach** first — your existing network. Cheapest, fastest
   feedback. ACA framework: Acknowledge personally, Compliment with a
   character label, Ask the question. Reach out to 100 people.
2. **Content** second — give away what others charge for. Builds
   inbound interest while you sleep, but slow to compound.
3. **Cold Outreach** third — outbound to strangers. Higher volume
   discipline required (YC: to get 1 customer you may need 800 emails).
4. **Paid Ads** last — scales fastest but loses fastest. Don't run
   paid before you can convert organic — you'll just burn cash on a
   broken funnel.

**Skok's GTM-Model lens (CAC scales 10× per touch level):**
freemium/PLG (~$400 CAC) → inside sales (~$5K) → field sales (~$50K).
Pick the lowest-touch model that fits your ACV. Selling a $99/mo
product via field sales kills unit economics from day one.

**Onboarding-Q10 connection.** The user already told us in onboarding
which channels they tried and which is working best
(`path_state.current_best_channel`). If a channel is working, default to
deepening that one — not to switching. Switch only if the current best
is structurally wrong for the ACV (e.g. cold-calling a $20/mo SaaS).

### 5. Sales Motion — one motion, written down

The sales motion is *how the deal moves from first touch to closed-won*,
stage by stage. By end of 2.1 it must be writeable on one page:

- Lead source → first touch
- First touch → discovery call
- Discovery → demo
- Demo → proposal
- Proposal → close
- Close → onboarding handoff

Each transition has: trigger criteria, owner, artifact, expected
duration, conversion rate. The conversion-rate column is what separates
"this is repeatable" from "we got lucky on three deals."

## Founder-led selling is non-negotiable

Phase 2.1 is **founder-led discovery, not delegated sales.** The role
is best filled by founders plus optionally one or two
**pathfinder/trailblazer reps** — people who help create and evolve the
playbook. Hiring an ordinary rep into 2.1 fails almost every time.

YC's reasoning: *"If you can't sell your own product, hiring someone
else won't fix it. Technical founders have a hidden advantage —
expertise and conviction."* Kazanjy's reasoning: *"The founder's job is
to bake the sales software in their own brain so it can later be
transplanted into other sellers' brains."* You cannot transplant
software that doesn't exist yet.

**Volume target:** Skok and Kazanjy both recommend ~50 to ~150 real
buyer conversations during this phase, with strangers (not just your
network). Kazanjy specifically: 100-150 conversations in 2 months,
funded by paid search or built outbound velocity to 15-20 conversations
per week.

## Exit criteria — the playbook test

Phase 2.1 is done when:

1. Multiple ICP-fit customers are happy and getting the promised benefit
2. The sales motion is written down stage by stage (one target market,
   one use case, one channel, who to call, what to say, pricing)
3. A stranger could read the document and execute it
4. At least 5–10 wins — same ICP, same message, same motion, same
   channel — closed the same way as each other

The fourth criterion is the "repeatable" test. 5–10 sales by the same
process is the minimum signal that the motion is real and not luck.
Two or three sales is anecdote. Ten deals via three different processes
is also anecdote.

## Anti-patterns

- **"We serve multiple personas."** The whole point of 2.1 is to pick
  one. Multiple personas means no playbook is repeatable for any of
  them.
- **Hiring a sales rep before the playbook exists.** Pathfinder reps
  CAN help in 2.1; ordinary reps WILL fail. Skok: "the single most
  expensive sales mistake a startup makes."
- **Mistaking warm leads for repeatable demand.** The Zaius case
  study (Mark Gally): warm leads from the founder's network closed,
  the team thought they had a motion, ramped sales hires — but no
  consistent segment, six-month enterprise cycle, three months of
  cash left. Recovery took a hard pivot.
- **Trying multiple channels in parallel.** Hormozi: "Pick one. Run
  it to a result. Then add a second."
- **Generic messaging.** "Powered by AI", "automated insights",
  "next-generation platform" — same site as every competitor.
- **Skipping the negative ICP.** Without it, sales says yes to every
  fit-ish lead. CS pays the price in 2.4.

## Connection to other steps

- **From PMF (Phase 1):** You arrive with Sean Ellis ≥ 40% and an
  HXC persona. The HXC informs but does not replace the ICP — HXC is
  about *who loves the product*, ICP is about *who will pay
  predictably and not churn*.
- **To 2.2 (Non-founders sell):** The playbook from 2.1 is the
  artifact you hand to the first ordinary rep. If 2.1 is undone, 2.2
  cannot start.
- **To 2.3 (Make growth scalable):** The single channel from 2.1
  becomes the first of *layered* lead sources in 2.3. 2.1 picks one,
  2.3 adds the next.
- **To 2.5 (Make it profitable):** The price point and channel-CAC
  set in 2.1 are what 2.5 will validate against the LTV:CAC > 3 and
  CAC-payback < 18mo gates.
