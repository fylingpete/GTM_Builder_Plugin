## Interviewing Salespeople — Matrix Partners (Stephanie Schatz)

Salespeople know how to present. They've been hired and re-hired on the strength of their interview performance. The interviewer has to dig past the polish.

### The Opening Question

After greeting and small talk, sit back and ask:

> "Great. I'd love to learn more about you. Where were you born, and what's happened since then?"

Then **stay silent**. The candidate has the stage. What you're testing:

- **Succinctness** — a great rep makes the answer crisp
- **Tailoring** — they read the room and emphasize what's relevant to your role
- **Reading you** — they pause if you look bored and pivot

If after three or four minutes they're still describing high-school sports, you've learned everything you need. *If they're boring you now, they'll bore your customers.*

### Find Proven Success — Not Theoretical Considerations

Past performance is the strongest signal, and it's the part candidates obscure most. Two failure modes to watch for:

- **Drift to theoretical** — "Well, what I would do now if I had that role again is..." Stop them. You want what they actually did.
- **Drift to "we" / "the company"** — "We had a great quarter that year." Many people were in the right place at the right time. Make sure they personally know *why* it worked.

Always ask: *"Why do you think this worked?"* and *"Why do you think this didn't work?"* Self-aware answers about failure are more diagnostic than success stories.

### Strengths and Weaknesses

Lead with: *"Apologies for the typical interview question, but tell me about your weaknesses and strengths."*

You're looking for **clear self-awareness** of strengths relevant to the role, **open communication** about weaknesses, and a **plan to counteract** them.

### Enthusiasm Test

> "What do you do when you don't work?"

You want genuine enthusiasm — for any topic. Brewing beer, kids, hiking, music. Whatever it is, the same enthusiasm will come through to your customers. Flat answers signal flat customer calls.

### Decision-Maker Test

End with: *"Do you have any questions for me?"*

You're testing whether they treat you (the decision maker) well. Look for:
- **Prepared questions** — not visibly improvised
- **Respectful of your time** — succinct
- **Relevant to your role** — not Google-able
- **No flattery** ("I saw you online and it looks so awesome") — that's a tell

### Sales Chops Tests

- "Tell me about a deal you won."
- "Tell me about a deal you lost — and why."
- "Tell me about the biggest deal you ever closed." *(Any real rep knows this number. They have war stories.)*
- "What makes a great salesperson?" *(Many right answers; matters that they have one.)*
- "What books have you read on sales?" *(Solution Selling, Challenger Sale, etc. Names matter.)*
- "Which sales methodology do you follow?" *(Many good ones; matters that they follow one.)*

## Key Takeaway
Test storytelling with the open-ended biography question, dig for concrete past actions (not theory), and probe self-awareness, enthusiasm, decision-maker handling, and sales fundamentals.

## When to Apply
- Interviewing the first VP Sales or Account Executive
- Building a sales-hiring scorecard
- Past sales hires looked great in interviews and underperformed in role
