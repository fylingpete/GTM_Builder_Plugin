## Stage-Appropriate SaaS Metrics — Matrix Partners (David Skok)

The SaaS business model is unusually sensitive to small changes in a few variables, but the variables that matter change with the phase. Obsessing over CAC and LTV at $100K ARR is one of the most common founder mistakes — there isn't enough data to calculate them meaningfully and the focus distracts from finding the repeatable motion.

### PMF Phase (1-3) — Engagement and KPI Change

Don't track CAC/LTV. Do track:

- **Product engagement** — instrument the product to see who is using which features. Tools like Mixpanel, Amplitude, or in-house analytics work. The signal you want is whether customers actually engage after sign-up.
- **The customer's KPI** — what number in the customer's business are you trying to change? (Time to develop an app. Sales-funnel conversion rate. Cost per ticket.) Measure whether you're actually moving it. This becomes the strongest renewal weapon later: when a champion leaves, you can prove the value to whoever's left by showing the KPI shift.

It's *useful* to speculate about what CAC might be at a given price point, but don't track it as a managed metric.

### Repeatable Growth (4-7) — Bookings Components

The single most important chart: **Net New ARR with its three components broken out**, plotted as a time series with planned-versus-actual lines:

- **New ARR from new customers** — are you finding and acquiring new accounts?
- **Expansion ARR** — are existing customers growing? *(Don't worry too much about expansion in early days — it's a later-stage phenomenon.)*
- **Churned ARR** — losses
- **Net new ARR (sum of above)** — the headline bookings line

Almost no early-stage SaaS pitch deck shows this — most show ARR. Without these four lines as a time series, you cannot run a SaaS business.

### Make-It-Profitable (8) — Full Unit Economics

Now CAC, LTV, LTV:CAC, time-to-recover-CAC, and gross margin enter the picture. By this phase, the data is statistically meaningful and the metrics drive real decisions about hiring pace and channel mix.

### Scaling (9+) — Add Forecasting Math

Reverse-engineer from desired bookings to required leads. Example: 4 closed deals per rep × 25 MQLs per closed deal = 100 MQLs per rep. With 10 reps, that's 1,000 MQLs from marketing. This becomes the **contract between sales and marketing** — the single best fix for the perpetual sales-vs-marketing blame game.

## Key Takeaway
The metrics that matter change with the phase — engagement first, bookings components next, full unit economics only when scaling — and obsessing over CAC pre-PMF wastes attention.

## When to Apply
- Building the first metrics dashboard for the leadership team
- Investor or board pressure to report CAC/LTV before there's data
- Deciding what numbers to display to the team day-to-day
