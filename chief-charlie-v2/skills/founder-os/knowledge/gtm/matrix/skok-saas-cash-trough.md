## The SaaS Cash-Flow Trough — Phase 8, Matrix Partners (David Skok)

GAAP accounting was built for one-time-sale businesses. It assigns no value to the recurring revenue stream that hasn't been billed yet. So a healthy SaaS company that spends heavily to acquire a customer (CAC) and recovers that cost over 12-18 months will look — to GAAP — like a money-losing business.

The faster a SaaS company grows, the worse this looks. Every new customer adds CAC up front and revenue spread over years. Plotting cumulative cash:

- Slow growth → shallow trough, recovers quickly
- Fast growth → deep trough, recovers later
- Doubling growth rate roughly **doubles the depth** of the trough

NetSuite went through three separate cash-flow troughs as they did things like international expansion. Each new growth investment dipped them back into significant losses before the cumulative recurring revenue caught up.

This is why investors panic. At HubSpot, Matrix Partners' partners were freaking out when the company was losing $10-15M per year and losses were *growing*. The intuition from non-SaaS businesses says "this is a death spiral." For a healthy SaaS business, it's the opposite — losses growing means growth is accelerating.

### Unit Economics — The Diagnostic Tool

Unit economics — CAC, LTV, LTV:CAC ratio, time to recover CAC, gross margin — answers a single question: *"Are you growing toward profitability or away from it?"*

Skok's guidelines (set ~10 years ago, validated by time):
- **LTV:CAC > 3** — the customer's lifetime value pays back acquisition cost at least 3x
- **Time to recover CAC < 12-18 months** — *(originally 12 months; loosened to 18 because SaaS capital is more available, but every month closer to 12 dramatically improves business quality)*
- **Gross margin > 70%** — *(Skok used to be sloppy about this assuming all SaaS had high margins. Not true. High onboarding costs and human-touch customer support eat margin. Either charge for those or automate them out.)*

### The Aggressive-Spending Implication

Companies with payback periods of 6-7 months are **not being aggressive enough**. They could spend significantly more on acquisition and still recover within healthy bounds. Tighter payback = headroom to invest harder.

### Segment-Based LTV:CAC

HubSpot in the early days had:
- **Direct sales to VSBs** (very small businesses): LTV:CAC = 1.5 — terrible
- **Sales through VARs** (value-added resellers) to the *same VSBs*: LTV:CAC = 5

The team had **12 reps on the bad segment, 4 on the good segment**. Once they ran the analysis, within a year they had **2 reps on the bad segment and 25 on the good**. Segment-by-segment unit economics is a powerful re-allocation tool — but you have to compute it.

## Key Takeaway
The SaaS cash trough deepens with faster growth — unit economics (LTV:CAC, CAC payback, gross margin) proves whether the trough is healthy or fatal, and segment-by-segment analysis reallocates investment to the highest-LTV channels.

## When to Apply
- Investors or board freaking out at growing losses
- Considering whether to spend more aggressively on acquisition
- Multiple customer segments and unsure which to invest behind
