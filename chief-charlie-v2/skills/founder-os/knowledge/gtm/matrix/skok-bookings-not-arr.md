## Bookings, Not Revenue or ARR — Phase 4, Matrix Partners (David Skok)

The most misleading SaaS chart is rising ARR with flat bookings. ARR is a stock — a backward-looking sum of all retained subscriptions. **Bookings is the flow** — net new ARR added in a given period:

```
Bookings = Net New ARR = New + Expansion − Churned
```

If bookings are flat from quarter to quarter, ARR still rises (because old subscriptions are still paying), but the line slope is gentle and linear. The business is dying in slow motion. If bookings are growing quarter over quarter, ARR rises with an accelerating curve — exponential growth visible in the chart. The two pictures look completely different even though ARR exists in both.

The diagnostic implication: when assessing whether the search for a repeatable, scalable, profitable growth model is working, **always plot quarterly bookings, not ARR**. The signal you are looking for is bookings going up over time. If bookings are flat, you have not yet found the repeatable motion — adding more reps will not fix it.

A second implication: bookings is the right number to compensate sales on. ARR over-rewards reps for keeping existing accounts (already counted in last year's bookings) and under-rewards them for the actual job — adding net new ARR.

A third implication: in fundraising narratives, growth-stage investors look at bookings trajectory, not ARR. They are evaluating whether the cash machine produces more output each quarter when more cash is fed in.

## Key Takeaway
Plot bookings, not ARR — flat bookings reveal a stalled business that ARR will hide for several quarters.

## When to Apply
- Reviewing growth metrics with the board or leadership team
- Designing sales compensation for the next plan
- Telling the company's growth story to investors or recruits
