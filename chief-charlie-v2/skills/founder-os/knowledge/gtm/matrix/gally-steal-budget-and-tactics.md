## Practitioner Tactics from Zaius — Matrix Partners (Mark Gally)

Four field-tested tactics from Mark Gally's recovery of Zaius.

### 1. The Undercover-Boss Test for Non-Founder Selling

Before hiring sales reps, **test whether someone who isn't the founder/CEO can sell the product** — using an alias.

Gally creates a fake email address and persona ("Steve Williams") and books his own demos under that name. The reason: prospects react differently to a CEO than to a sales rep. If you're selling a $5K product and the CEO is doing the demo, prospects subconsciously expect more, and the deal dynamics distort.

By running the demo in disguise, the founder gets a clean read on whether the motion works without the CEO halo. If it doesn't, the playbook needs more work *before* you hire actual reps.

### 2. Hire Two Reps in Cohorts of Two

Gally followed Skok/Schatz's "hire two at the same time" rule and adds a wrinkle: **make them different profiles**.

For Zaius:
- **Trailblazer** — from a higher-ASP company in the same market, brought evangelistic muscle
- **Grinder** — from a lower-ASP, ~30-day cycle company; their previous job mixed selling with customer success / managing accounts post-sale

Both ramped on the same playbook but exposed different parts of the motion. The pair revealed whether the playbook was robust or whether one specific profile was the only one who could close.

He continues to hire in cohorts of two or more.

### 3. Engineering Team as Volunteer Fire Department

Early Zaius launched a freemium tier and got hundreds of customers. Onboarding broke immediately — no pre-built integrations, customers struggling to upload spreadsheets, no smooth path to value.

Solution: **the engineering team rotated through customer success.** Engineers personally onboarded customers, watched the friction, felt the spreadsheet pain. Internal name: "the volunteer fire department running customer success."

Result: engineering shipped pre-built integrations and simplified the customizable dashboard down to a relevant default view — both directly informed by experiencing the pain. Far better than reading bug reports.

### 4. Steal an Existing Budget, Don't Create a New One

When evaluating whether a market is scalable, ask: *where does the budget for our product come from?* Two options:

- **Create a new budget** — convince buyers to allocate fresh money for a new line item. Hard. Slow. Many no's.
- **Steal an existing budget** — show up at a moment when the buyer is reallocating budget for an adjacent category, and provide more value with overlap into your category.

Zaius didn't try to convince B2C marketers to fund a new "data-first marketing automation" budget. They positioned to capture the **email-service-provider refresh cycle** — a moment when buyers are already rethinking infrastructure and have budget in motion. Zaius framed: "We send emails too, but we solve the bigger problem of *what's next* with your data."

Same product, dramatically different sales motion. The trigger event made everything possible.

### 5. Multiple Wow Moments for Non-Founder Selling

When the founder is the salesperson, one wow moment is often enough — the founder's narrative carries the rest. When non-founder reps demo, **the product itself has to carry the wow**. Zaius shifted demos from one wow to a sequence: lifecycle visualization → customer profile drill-down → revenue-anchored analytics dashboards. Each wow reinforces the next, and the rep doesn't have to be a charismatic founder for the demo to land.

## Key Takeaway
Test handoff with an alias, hire reps in cohorts of two with mixed profiles, rotate engineering through CS, steal existing budgets via trigger events, and stack multiple wow moments so the product (not the founder) carries the demo.

## When to Apply
- About to hand sales over from founder to ordinary reps
- Onboarding is breaking and you're not sure why
- Trying to figure out why the GTM motion is slow or stalled
