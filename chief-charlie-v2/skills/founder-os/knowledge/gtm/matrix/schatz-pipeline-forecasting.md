## Pipeline Management and Forecasting — Matrix Partners (Stephanie Schatz)

Once a startup is in phase 6, hitting the number every quarter is the only thing that matters. The infrastructure for that is pipeline discipline.

### Clearly Defined Opportunity Stages

Every customer journey must move through documented stages with **exit criteria**. Example: an opportunity cannot move into "Negotiation" until the customer has actually responded to the proposal with comments on the numbers. *"If I just send you a proposal, we're not negotiating — I'm just talking at you."*

Without exit criteria, reps move stage labels around to look productive. With exit criteria, the pipeline reflects reality.

### Pipeline Meetings — Strict but Fun

Pipeline meetings happen every week. They should be:
- **Strict** — reps know their deals; Salesforce is clean; otherwise the meeting can't function
- **Demanding** — every customer must have moved one stage closer to closing or be flagged as stuck
- **Fun** — boring or aggressive meetings kill morale; honesty thrives in psychologically safe rooms

Have reps stand up in front of the team to present their accounts. The implicit goal: *make your teammates proud.* "Wow, Kelly really knows her accounts — she has the cell phone of every decision-maker." That's the bar.

### No Sandbagging — Except by the Sales Leader

Reps must call deals honestly. **One person, and one person only, adjusts numbers up or down: the sales leader.** And they tell the CEO openly: "Rep is calling 2M commit; I see 1.7M; we're going to land at 1.7M."

When multiple people sandbag in different directions ("hedging" or "bolstering"), the sales leader misses the number and the CEO misses with the board. Total honesty, single point of adjustment.

### Forecast Stages — Commit Means Commit

Define explicit forecast stages, e.g.:
- **20% — Possible**
- **80% — Probable**
- **100% — Commit** *(In enterprise sales, signed in blood. The rep is staking their credibility on this deal closing this period.)*

Exception: in high-volume SMB motions where each rep handles 200-500 accounts, "commit" might be 90% with a calibrated buffer.

### Never Rely on a Weighted Forecast

The most common forecast failure: averaging probabilities to invent certainty. Five 100K deals at 20% does **not** equal 100K guaranteed. It equals five maybes.

The right structure is two lists:
1. **Base goal commit list** — deals you know are happening, with the steps you're tracking weekly
2. **Upside list** — best-case deals that, if they close, get you to stretch

Hit the base, treat upside as upside.

### Why Forecast Accuracy Matters

Once a company is public or close to it, missing the number is bad — but **landing way above committed numbers is also bad** because it signals you don't understand your business. Eight quarters of running operations for a 380-rep public company taught: the street punishes both directions.

For private companies, accuracy compounds credibility with the board. Some leaders pay reps a small percent on **forecast accuracy** itself — sharpens the discipline.

## Key Takeaway
Stage discipline, exit criteria, single-point sandbagging at the sales leader, commit-means-commit, and base-goal + upside lists — never a weighted average — produce reliable forecasts.

## When to Apply
- Pipeline meetings are unstructured or feel theatrical
- Forecasts have been wrong in either direction for multiple quarters
- Building the cadence for a scaling sales team
