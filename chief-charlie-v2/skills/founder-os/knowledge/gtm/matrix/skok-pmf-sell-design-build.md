## Sell, Design, Build — Not Design, Build, Sell — Phase 1, Matrix Partners (David Skok)

The most common pattern in B2B startups: technical or product-driven founders design what they think the market needs, build it, and only then try to sell it. The result is an MVP that took 6-18 months and a market reaction of "interesting, but not for us." The cash is gone and the founders are demoralized.

The right sequence is the opposite. **Sell first** — try to convince real buyers to pay (or at least commit to evaluate) for a clear value proposition. **Then design** the product around what they actually need. **Then build** the minimum that proves value.

Selling first surfaces three things that no amount of internal whiteboarding can: which target market actually has the pain, how they describe the problem (their words become your messaging), and what features genuinely matter versus what is internal-team imagination.

The reason this advice is so hard to follow: founders from technical or product backgrounds are uncomfortable with prospecting and cold outreach. They prefer the work they know — building. The two practical ways to lower the friction of "sell first" without doing pure cold calls are **LinkedIn outreach** (warm intros, targeted by ICP) and **Google AdWords** (paid search to attract people already searching for the problem you'd solve).

The Levitt principle applies: people don't want a quarter-inch drill, they want a quarter-inch hole. Selling first forces you to learn what hole the buyer needs to drill — before you spend a year building the wrong drill.

## Key Takeaway
Sell first, design second, build third — every reversal of this order costs months of runway and produces products no one wants.

## When to Apply
- About to start building an MVP without having had 50+ buyer conversations
- Technical founder avoiding the discomfort of prospecting
- Stuck on what to build because the team has too many feature ideas
