## Practical Buyer-Persona Questions — Phase 4, Matrix Partners (David Skok)

Most buyer-persona templates stop at demographics. The useful version goes into the buyer's job, pain, search behavior, and reactions. Five clusters of questions:

### 1. Identifying characteristics

- Industry / vertical
- Company size and tech stack
- Special characteristics (e.g. selling to developers means assuming they prefer open source, dislike paying, but have huge influence over operations spending — JBoss learned to use developers as a channel to operations buyers, not as the buyers themselves)

### 2. Professional role

- What does a day in their life look like?
- What are their key business goals?
- What does their boss expect of them?
- How does our product help them achieve that?
- What are their personal motivations and aspirations?
- What would make their job easier?

### 3. Pain

- What pain do they have? Is it **latent** (they don't recognize it yet — must educate first) or **obvious** (they're already searching)?
- How do they describe it in their own words? *(Their words become your messaging.)*
- Is solving it a high priority? If not, what features or events would make it one?
- What **triggers** cause them to buy? (lost data → backup; new project → new dev tools; security audit → APM, etc.)

### 4. Search behavior

- When they search for a solution, where do they go?
- Sites: G2, Bombora, Capterra
- Influencers or bloggers in the category
- Google searches with which keywords
- Communities, conferences, Slack groups

This map tells marketing exactly **where to show up** in the buyer's awareness phase.

### 5. Reaction to your product

- Decision criteria they will apply
- What they will like
- What they will not like (objections, missing features, cultural fit issues)
- Which competitors they'll also evaluate, and what they'll like or dislike about each

This list directly feeds the sales playbook — battlecards, objection handling, and demo scripts.

### Why this matters

Personas built around demographics produce generic marketing. Personas built around triggers, search behavior, and reactions produce campaigns that intercept the buyer at the moment of stage transition. That's the difference between content that "feels nice" and content that converts.

## Key Takeaway
A useful buyer persona answers what triggers them to buy, where they search, and how they will react to your product — not just demographics.

## When to Apply
- Building marketing campaigns that aren't converting
- Writing the sales playbook and battlecards for the first time
- Trying to identify trigger events that signal buying intent
