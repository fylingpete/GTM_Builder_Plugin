## Negative Churn — The SaaS Accelerant — Phase 8, Matrix Partners (David Skok)

Customer churn rate and dollar churn rate are different numbers. With two customers — one at $5K and one at $1K:
- Lose customer 1 (smaller): 50% customer churn, only 17% dollar churn
- Lose customer 2 (larger): 50% customer churn, 83% dollar churn — much worse

Track both, but **dollar renewal rate is king**.

### What is Negative Churn?

If existing customers expand more than departing customers shrink, dollar churn goes negative — you keep growing even with zero new bookings. Same example: if you lose customer 1 ($1K) but expand customer 2 from $5K to $7K, you have **+33% net dollar retention**. Negative dollar churn (i.e., NDR > 100%) is the single most powerful compounding factor in SaaS.

The math at scale is brutal. At $100M ARR with 2.5% positive monthly churn, you lose ~$30M/year just to hold steady — finding $30M of new bookings just to break even is painful. With 2.5% **negative** monthly churn, the same revenue base ends a year at ~$400K (versus $150K with positive churn) — almost 3x the same input.

### The Pricing Prerequisite

You cannot get negative churn with one fixed price. HubSpot in the early days had one price (~$5-6K) for everyone. Zero ability to expand customers. They had to redesign pricing with **variable axes** — number of leads as the metering axis. This is the work to do *before* you reach phase 8, because changing pricing on existing customers is hard.

Common variable axes:
- Seats / users
- Usage volume (leads, API calls, storage, contacts)
- Edition tiers (Starter / Professional / Enterprise)
- Feature add-ons (security pack, integrations pack)

### The High-End Customer Tactic

If a small fraction of customers are willing to pay 100x your average deal size but you only sell at average, segment them. Find what's different — usually security, reliability, or specific integrations like Active Directory. **Build an "Enterprise" version with those features at 5-10x the price.** Name it so high-end buyers self-identify ("I'm an enterprise, I want the Enterprise tier"). They walk up the pricing menu themselves.

### Two Causes of Churn (and What to Do)

After many portfolio-company conversations, two patterns dominate:

1. **Onboarding failure.** A customer who never got the product working is impossible to re-engage at renewal. Measure onboarding success — get the customer to confirm they're fully productive at the end of the onboarding period.

2. **Champion left.** The original buyer is gone and the new person doesn't know why they're paying. This is hard to measure but possible: check quarterly whether the champion is still in role, and if not, **resell the account** — find a new champion or arm the budget-holder with the KPI proof of value.

## Key Takeaway
Negative dollar churn is the biggest growth accelerant in SaaS, but it requires variable pricing axes designed early — and onboarding success and champion-tracking to prevent churn at all.

## When to Apply
- Designing or redesigning pricing during phase 6-8
- High-value customers exist but the average deal size is far smaller
- Renewals are missing because customers' original buyer left
