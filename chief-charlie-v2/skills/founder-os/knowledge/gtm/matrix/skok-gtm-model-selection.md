## Choosing a Go-to-Market Model — Phase 4, Matrix Partners (David Skok)

CAC does not grow linearly with sales complexity — it grows **exponentially**. Three real data points:

- **Constant Contact** (light-touch inside sales): ~$400 CAC
- **HubSpot** (medium-touch inside sales): ~$5,000 CAC
- **Enterprise field sales** (BI products at $1M+ deal sizes): ~$50,000 CAC

Each step up in sales touch is roughly a **10x jump in CAC**. Plotted on a log scale, this is a clean staircase.

The implication: any added human touch in the sales process is significant cost. Avoid it where you can. The exception is when deal size justifies the spend (a $1M deal can fund $50K of CAC and still produce great unit economics).

### The Salesforce-vs-Siebel Disruption Pattern

Siebel forced on-premise installation, IT involvement, and on-site POCs. Salesforce eliminated all of it — credit-card SaaS access, no IT touch. Same buyer, same problem, dramatically different sales motion → Siebel got disrupted.

Ask of your category: **can a competitor remove the human touch you're forced to use?** If yes, they will, and they'll beat you on price and motion.

### Why Long Sales Cycles Are Especially Bad for Startups

Beyond the CAC cost, long cycles mean **few learning cycles**. A startup with a 30-day cycle closing 10 deals/month has 10 learning iterations monthly. A startup with a 9-month cycle closing 2 deals/quarter has ~2 iterations every 3 months. The first startup will out-learn and out-evolve the second.

### Sources of Sales Complexity

Common drivers (each adds complexity, length, and CAC):
- High price point
- Difficult to evaluate (requires POC, integration with multiple systems)
- Multiple people involved in the buying decision
- Risk to the buyer's business if the product fails
- Requires bundled purchase of other products or services

For each one, ask: can the product, pricing, or packaging be redesigned to remove it?

### Land-and-Expand and Product-Qualified Leads (PQL)

The best model when achievable: **land cheaply, expand inside the customer.** Slack, Dropbox, Atlassian.

Christopher O'Donnell at HubSpot defined the **PQL** (Product Qualified Lead) — replacing the MQL with usage-based qualification:
- User signed up → low buying intent
- User engaged with the product → medium intent
- User tripped a feature limit (e.g. Dropbox 2GB) → ready to pay
- User self-upgraded with credit card → no human touch needed

Designing the product to surface PQL signals dramatically improves CAC efficiency.

### Freemium Health Warning

Freemium can work, but only when it brings the right buyers. If freemium attracts users who will never be in your ICP, you're spending support and infrastructure on people you can't monetize. Several companies have started freemium and turned it off when the cost outweighed the conversion.

### The Salesforce vs Atlassian Spend Mix

Salesforce: 21% R&D, 66% sales & marketing
Atlassian: 55% R&D, 25% sales & marketing

The Atlassian model creates a **virtuous cycle**: more R&D → better products → less need for sales interactions → more R&D. Customers don't want sales interactions; they want products. Spending on what they want compounds.

### When to Pick the Model

Don't panic about picking too early. Bring in even an expensive salesperson to get the first selling engagements going — you'll learn what causes complexity and where simplification opportunities live. Then evolve toward less touch.

Sales reps aren't going away even in PQL models — they're needed to expand initial land points into bigger contracts higher in the org. The job changes; it doesn't disappear.

## Key Takeaway
CAC scales exponentially with sales touch — design land-and-expand or PQL motions to avoid the 10x jumps wherever the deal size doesn't fund the touch.

## When to Apply
- Choosing or redesigning the go-to-market motion
- Considering freemium and unsure if it fits
- CAC is creeping up and you're wondering if a different model is needed
