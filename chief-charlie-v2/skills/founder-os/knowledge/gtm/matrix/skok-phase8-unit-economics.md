## Phase 8 — Make it Profitable: Unit Economics Gates — Phase 8, Matrix Partners (David Skok)

By phase 8 the company should be producing growth that is repeatable and scalable. Phase 8 asks: is it profitable? Three unit-economics thresholds gate the answer:

### LTV : CAC > 3

Lifetime Value of a customer divided by Cost to Acquire that customer must exceed 3x. Below 3x, growth investments don't produce enough margin to support the rest of the company (R&D, G&A, leadership). At 3x and above, every dollar spent acquiring a customer returns at least three over their lifetime, leaving room for everything else.

### Time to Recover CAC < 12-18 months

Even if LTV:CAC is 5, a 36-month payback means cash is locked up for three years per customer. SaaS companies funding growth from cash flow need payback inside ~12 months. Venture-funded companies can stretch to ~18. Beyond that, growth requires constantly raising money to fund the acquisition gap.

This metric also reveals subtle issues: if payback is creeping up over quarters, CAC is rising or ARR per customer is falling — both flag-worthy.

### Gross Margins > 70%

SaaS gross margin is the test of whether the business is fundamentally a software business. Below 70% suggests heavy services dependency, infrastructure inefficiency, or a delivery model that can't scale. Public SaaS companies trade based largely on gross margin times growth rate (the Rule of 40 cousin: EV/Gross-Profit, not EV/Revenue, is the better predictor of valuation per Jared Sleeper's analysis).

### Implications

If any of the three gates fails, fix it before phase 9. Hitting the gas with a 50% gross margin or 30-month CAC payback magnifies losses and burns the cash machine into bankruptcy. The temptation to keep growing through the gates is the most common phase 8 mistake.

## Key Takeaway
LTV:CAC > 3, CAC payback < 12-18 months, gross margin > 70% — fix any failing gate before scaling, because scaling magnifies bad unit economics.

## When to Apply
- Reviewing the financial model before a fundraise
- Considering increasing sales and marketing investment
- Investors flagged unit-economics concerns in a board meeting
