## Conserve Cash Until the Scaling Phase — Matrix Partners (David Skok)

The duration of the PMF phase and the Repeatable-Growth phase is **fundamentally unpredictable**. One portfolio company shipped a product without enough customer development and took two years before traction appeared. That unpredictability has one practical implication: keep burn as low as possible until phase 8 is complete.

The pattern is counter-intuitive. While founders are searching for PMF and a repeatable growth model (phases 1-8), every dollar they don't spend buys them more time to find what works. Once they've found it (phase 9+), the calculus inverts — they should spend aggressively, even pull cash forward through fundraising, because they now have a proven cash machine and B2B markets tend toward winner-takes-all dynamics.

The most common cash mistake during phases 1-8 is hiring salespeople before phase 5 is complete, or scaling sales before phase 6 is complete. Both are seductive because boards and investors push for visible "growth", but the cash burn shortens runway without producing results.

The mirror-image mistake happens at phase 9: founders stay in burn-avoidance mode and fail to invest hard enough to win. The HubSpot board took an hour of debate to convince the leadership team to start hiring two salespeople every month once they had reached repeatable scale — the mental switch was that hard.

The diagnostic question to ask at every board meeting: **"When will we run out of cash, and what milestones do we need to hit by three months before that point to have a successful next fundraise?"**

## Key Takeaway
Cash burn should be low through phases 1-8 and aggressive in phase 9 — getting either side of that wrong is what kills B2B startups.

## When to Apply
- Planning the next fundraise and trying to set milestones
- Tempted to hire salespeople or run paid ads to "show progress"
- Just reached phase 9 and the team is reluctant to spend aggressively
