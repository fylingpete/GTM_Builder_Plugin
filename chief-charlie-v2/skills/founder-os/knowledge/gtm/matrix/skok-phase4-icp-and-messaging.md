## Ideal Customer Profile and Messaging — Phase 4, Matrix Partners (David Skok)

Two artifacts must come out of phase 4 and be written down: the ICP and the core messaging. Both are wrong if they aren't specific enough to disqualify prospects on sight.

### Ideal Customer Profile (ICP)

The ICP has two halves — what to look for and what to avoid:

**Ideal Prospect Characteristics**
- Industry
- Use case
- Company size
- Job function
- Key characteristics (e.g. "uses Salesforce", "has a sales-led motion", "raised Series B in last 18 months")

**Negative Characteristics** — disqualifiers that signal the prospect will not be a fit. Examples: companies in heavily regulated industries, sub-50-employee companies, customers without an existing CRM, founders who want unlimited customization. The negative list is what stops sales from wasting cycles on bad-fit deals.

The discipline of writing both halves protects the rep's time and the customer success team's sanity later. Every "no, this is not our ICP" decision in phase 4 saves churn pain in phase 7.

### Messaging Rules

Five rules for the core messaging artifact:
- **Business benefits, not product features**
- **Clear** — anyone in the buyer persona understands instantly
- **Simple** — no jargon, no internal vocabulary
- **Short** — fits on a single slide or homepage hero
- **Explains differentiation** — why this rather than the alternative

The Theodore Levitt principle (Harvard marketing professor) anchors all of it: *"People don't want to buy a quarter-inch drill. They want a quarter-inch hole."* The messaging should be about the hole the buyer needs to drill in their business — the outcome they're paying for — not the features of your drill.

### Use-Case-Specific Wins

Generic messaging like "automated insights from your data" loses to use-case-specific messaging like "automatically warns you when funnel indicators show a problem so you can fix issues before bookings drop." Same product, dramatically different conversion.

## Key Takeaway
Write the ICP with both positive and negative characteristics, and write benefit-led messaging about the buyer's outcome — generic positioning never closes deals.

## When to Apply
- Conversion rates from leads to opportunities are low
- Sales reps complain that "we close some deals but they're all different"
- Marketing copy feels generic and could be on any competitor's site
