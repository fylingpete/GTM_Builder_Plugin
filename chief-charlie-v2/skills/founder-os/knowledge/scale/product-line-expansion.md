# Add additional product lines

Step 3.4 is where compounding scaling SaaS companies either acquire a
second engine or quietly become single-product businesses dressed up
with adjacent feature releases. Skok's Phase 11 framing names three
valid expansion vectors — geographic (covered in 3.3), vertical-depth,
and second product. This step is the product-line vector, with
vertical-depth subsumed inside it as a sibling discipline. Both vectors
share the same disciplines (Core Focus™, NDR-precondition, sequencing,
kill criteria) and the same anti-patterns.

3.4 is also the **terminal step** of the Founder-OS Scale path. The
exit criteria are not "ready for Step 3.5" — there is no 3.5. Passing
3.4 puts the company in Operating Mode, where the cadences from 3.2
(L10™, Quarterly Rocks™, Annual Planning) continue as the operating
system but no new path is recommended. The skill treats this as a
graduation, not a continuation.

The hardest part of 3.4 is that the second product looks like a small
bolt-on from the home market's vantage point and looks like a startup-
from-zero from the new product's vantage point. Both views are correct.
The discipline of this step is treating it as a startup-inside-the-
startup with its own PMF risk, while leveraging the distribution and
brand the core product has built.

## The precondition — NDR > 110% sustained

The single most important precondition for a second product: net dollar
retention sustained above 110% on the core product. Below 110%, the
core product is leaking faster than the customer base is expanding, and
adding a second product is solving the wrong problem.

The mechanism: a second product that succeeds expands existing customers
(cross-sell adds to NDR). A second product launched into a base with
NDR < 100% just papers over the underlying churn for a quarter or two
before the churn shows up in the combined metric. The right move with
NDR < 100% is fixing the core product's retention, not adding products.

Skok's negative-dollar-churn math is the underlying physics. At $100M
ARR with 2.5% positive monthly churn, the company loses ~$30M/year just
to hold steady. With 2.5% negative monthly churn (NDR > 100%), the
same revenue base ends the year ~3× higher. The pricing axes that
enable expansion (seats, usage, tiers, add-ons) must be in place before
3.4 starts — pricing redesigns mid-flight at this scale are months of
pain.

## The three properties of a successful second product

YC's research and Skok's portfolio data converge on the same three
properties. Most second products at scaling SaaS companies fail to
reach 20% of total ARR; the ones that succeed share these properties:

**1. Same ICP.** The second product sells to the same buyer at the
same company. Different ICP means different sales motion, different
CSM playbook, different unit economics — at which point you are
starting a second startup, not extending the first. The 5,000-customer
installed base does not give the second product PMF; it gives it
distribution, which is a different thing.

**2. Same distribution channel.** The first product's installed base
or sales motion delivers the second product. If the second product
requires a new channel — paid ads for the first product, content for
the second — the channel-build costs eat the distribution advantage
and the second product becomes a financially-undisciplined startup
rather than a leveraged extension.

**3. Treated as a startup-inside-the-startup.** Dedicated team,
dedicated PM, dedicated GTM lead, own metrics, own roadmap, own
quarterly review. The CEO is the board member, not the operator.
Bolted onto the existing product team's roadmap, the second product
gets the leftover capacity and dies.

If any of the three is missing, the second product is unlikely to
succeed. If all three are present, the success rate is meaningfully
higher than starting a brand-new company — because distribution is
the hardest part of any startup, and the second product gets it
for free.

## Customer demand is necessary but not sufficient

"Customers are asking for it" is the signal founders point to most
often. It is necessary but not sufficient — customers ask for many
things, most of which, when built, are not used.

The validation pattern: pre-sell the second product to 10 customers at
the actual price before any engineering work begins. If 10 customers
won't put money down (real letters of intent, not "I'd consider it"),
the asks are polite, not real. This is the Phase 11 analog of the
Phase 1 demand-validation discipline — the disciplines that worked
when finding PMF the first time still apply to finding PMF the second
time.

## Core Focus™ — stay in the cone

Wickman's Core Focus™ is the EOS™ filter that prevents 3.4 from
becoming a shiny-object trap. The Core Focus™ has two sub-parts:
**Purpose / Cause / Passion** (the WHY beyond money) and **Niche**
(the ONE thing the company is exceptional at today). The intersection
of the two defines a "sweet spot" inside which the company is allowed
to play.

Every second-product candidate runs through the Core Focus™ filter
before it gets engineering resources:

- Does this strengthen the niche, or stray from it?
- Could the same leadership team naturally lead this, or does it
  require an entirely new leadership profile?
- Would the company's customers see this and say "of course you'd
  make this," or would they say "why are you making this?"

The further from the cone, the higher the failure probability. A
common test from the 3.2 install applies here: if the new product
requires hiring an entirely new leadership team with different domain
expertise, it is outside the cone.

This filter exists specifically to keep the most attractive-looking
opportunities from pulling the company off the compounding path. The
case for taking an off-cone opportunity always sounds reasonable in
the moment ("the margin is great," "the customer is great," "we'd be
crazy not to") — and the case is almost always wrong at this stage.

## Niche for pricing, not volume — the 10x rule

Hormozi's niching framework cuts in at the vertical-depth axis: the
same fundamental offer can be worth 100x more when narrowed from a
broad audience to a hyper-specific avatar. Pricing power comes from
specificity, not from work. At Phase 11, the company's existing niche
is producing diminishing returns; the alternative to a new product is
deepening the niche.

The discipline: **never make an adjacent move for a 2x. Only make
it for a 10x.** Broader markets are tempting but they dilute pricing
power. Only expand when the opportunity created by the move more than
replaces current revenue at the current niche.

Combined with the Core Focus™ filter: a 10x adjacent move that stays
in the cone is the move. A 10x adjacent move outside the cone is a
trap. A 2x adjacent move in the cone is not worth the leadership
attention.

## Vertical-depth — the alternative to a second product

The vertical-depth vector is the under-marketed sibling of second-
product expansion. Instead of building a new product, the company
deepens its position inside the existing niche: add features the niche
specifically asks for, deepen integrations with the niche's adjacent
tools, raise prices to match the value being delivered.

The defensibility move: become unrideable in the chosen vertical.
Customers in the niche cannot leave because no competitor matches the
combination of features, integrations, and reputation the company has
accumulated.

Vertical-depth is often the higher-EV choice when NDR is between
100% and 110% (room to grow inside the existing base) and the niche
hasn't been deepened in 18+ months. Pricing increases, expansion
modules, and integration partnerships compound retention without the
PMF risk of a new product.

Vertical-depth runs in parallel with a second product when both are
in the cone — not in conflict.

## M&A as a vector-buying option

At Phase 11, M&A becomes a viable lever — either from a growth-round
cash pile or from public-company stock as currency. M&A can buy a
vector: a second product, a geographic foothold, or a vertical
specialization, without the build risk.

The discipline: M&A is a vector decision, not a growth shortcut. The
same Core Focus™ filter applies — an off-cone acquisition is harder
to integrate, not easier. The same NDR > 110% precondition applies —
acquiring revenue while leaking the core base is the most expensive
shape of growth on paper.

M&A integration consumes 12-18 months of leadership attention.
Treated as "free growth," it almost always disappoints. Treated as a
deliberate vector choice with explicit integration milestones, the
M&A path produces results comparable to organic vector expansion
with a different risk profile.

## Set kill criteria pre-launch

Mirror of the 3.3 kill-criteria discipline applied to a second
product. Standard form: "If the second product does not show a Sean
Ellis-like PMF signal by month 18 (or alternative: cohort retention
> 70% at 6 months), we kill the product and reallocate the team."
Without kill criteria, second products linger in "promising but not
yet" for years, draining capital and leadership attention.

Paul Graham's framing: kill the second product the moment it stops
looking like the company's future. Don't let it become a zombie
product the team can't quite kill but also can't grow.

## Exit criteria — the Scale-Complete test

Step 3.4 is the terminal step. It is done when all six are true:

1. **One additional vector has been proven** — second product OR
   geographic country OR M&A acquisition — contributing ≥ 20% of total
   ARR with its own positive unit economics.
2. **Core Focus™ has held.** The expansion stayed inside the cone;
   off-cone opportunities were declined with the decisions documented.
3. **NDR ≥ 110% sustained for ≥ 4 consecutive quarters** across the
   combined product mix.
4. **Rule of 40 ≥ 40** sustained for ≥ 4 consecutive quarters. The
   cross-step KPI is now in the "compounding healthy" range.
5. **Organizational Checkup™ ≥ 80** sustained — the leadership team
   is running a multi-vector business without re-routing decisions
   through the founder.
6. **Kill criteria written and applied at least once** — at least one
   vector candidate (a country, a second product, or an acquisition)
   has been either killed or kept against pre-written kill criteria.

When all six are true, the Founder-OS Scale path is **complete**.
The company is in Operating Mode: the cadences (L10™ weekly, Monthly
Review, Quarterly Rocks™, Annual Planning + V/TO™ refresh + Org
Checkup™) continue indefinitely. The system has no Phase 4. This is
the terminal state.

## Anti-patterns

- **Adding a second product with NDR < 100% on the core.** Solving
  the wrong problem. Fix retention first.
- **Second product bolted onto the existing product team's roadmap.**
  Gets leftover capacity, dies in 12-18 months.
- **Different ICP for the second product.** That's a different
  company, not a second product. Either fully commit to running two
  startups or back out.
- **"Customers are asking for it" as the only validation.** Necessary,
  not sufficient. Pre-sell to 10 at the real price before any code is
  written.
- **Off-cone opportunities pursued because they look profitable.**
  The Core Focus™ filter exists specifically to decline these. Each
  off-cone yes erodes the company's identity.
- **2x adjacent expansion.** Dilutes pricing power without replacing
  current revenue. Only expand for 10x.
- **M&A as a growth shortcut.** Integration consumes 12-18 months
  of leadership attention. Treat M&A as a deliberate vector choice or
  decline.
- **No kill criteria, or retroactive kill criteria.** Second products
  become zombie products that drain resources for years.
- **CEO operating the second product personally.** Pre-launch this
  looks like the founder's commitment; post-launch it means the core
  product loses its operator. Day-one GM hire is the move.
- **Pursuing both 3.3 (geographic) and 3.4 (product-line)
  simultaneously.** Skok's sequencing discipline applies across
  vectors, not just within each. Sequential beats parallel.

## Connection to other steps

- **From 3.2:** The Visionary's Core Focus™ + the leadership team's
  operating cadence + the V/TO™'s 10-Year Target™ and 3-Year Picture™
  are the load-bearing artifacts. Without the 3.2 install, 3.4 cannot
  be governed.
- **From 3.1:** NDR > 110% is the precondition that 3.1 produced (or
  failed to produce). Pricing axes that enable expansion (seats, usage,
  tiers) are designed before 3.4 starts.
- **Parallel-okay with 3.3:** Both are valid Phase 11 vectors. A
  company can sequence them in either order, or run one and skip the
  other depending on what the home market signals. Don't run both
  simultaneously — pick one and prove it before the other.
- **To Operating Mode (terminal):** No path 4. Cadences from 3.2 +
  cross-step KPIs (Rule of 40, NDR, Leadership-Health-Score) continue
  as the operating system. The Founder-OS skill displays "Path
  Complete — Operating Mode" once the 3.4 exit criteria are met.
