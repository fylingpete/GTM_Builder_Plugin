## GWC™ — Get It, Want It, Capacity — EOS™ (Gino Wickman)

GWC™ is the three-part filter EOS™ uses to decide whether a specific person is in the right seat. It is the "right seat" half of Right Person, Right Seat — Core Values handle the "right person" half, and GWC handles seat fit. The rule that makes the filter useful is its binary nature: **all three have to be yes**. Two out of three is not a partial pass — it is a fail.

Tolerance for ambiguity on GWC is the single most common reason the rest of EOS quietly stops working. Wrong people in seats break the Accountability Chart™, and a broken chart breaks every downstream tool.

### Get it

Intuitive understanding of what the seat is actually for — its purpose inside the business, the rhythm of the work, how it connects to the rest of the organization. A person who gets it can be dropped into a conversation about the seat and immediately talk about it in a way that sounds right to other people who own similar seats. They don't have to be told what their job is, don't ask why they exist, don't get blindsided by the recurring patterns of the role.

**The hard part:** Get it cannot be reliably trained into someone who doesn't have it. Some people simply "see" a particular kind of role and some do not. Borderline cases can sometimes be coached across over months; someone who fundamentally does not get it cannot be converted by training or experience. The filter exists to surface that reality early.

### Want it

Whether the person genuinely wants to do this work, day after day, on its own terms, at fair compensation. Keyword: *genuinely*.

- Wanting the title is not wanting it
- Wanting the salary is not wanting it
- Tolerating it because the alternative seems worse is not wanting it
- Being talked into it by a manager who needs the seat filled is definitely not wanting it

Wickman warns specifically against the management instinct to persuade, motivate, or over-pay someone into a seat they do not actually want. That instinct is well-meaning and almost always backfires. People who do not want a job will eventually disengage regardless of how much the company is paying them.

### Capacity to do it

Multi-dimensional question of whether the person has the bandwidth across every dimension to deliver the seat's five major roles at the level the business needs:

- **Time** — the role might require 55 hours/week and the person only has 40
- **Mental bandwidth** — cognitive load, context-switching, strategic thinking
- **Emotional bandwidth** — resilience under conflict, delivering hard messages, interpersonal friction
- **Physical capacity** — stamina, presence
- **Skills** — technical or domain expertise not yet developed
- **Judgment** — consequential decisions under ambiguity

A "no" on capacity does NOT mean the person is lesser, weaker, or less valuable. It means the match between this specific person and this specific seat is wrong today. The right move is almost always a different seat, not termination.

### Why it has to be binary

Wickman insists on binary because the alternative is the slow death of every accountability process. A "mostly yes" on Get it becomes a culture of subtle confusion about each role. A "mostly yes" on Want it becomes the gradual disengagement of someone who can't quite name why they're tired. A "mostly yes" on Capacity becomes constant near-misses the team papers over until they can't anymore.

Binary discipline forces honesty earlier. Teams using GWC strictly surface seat-fit problems within weeks instead of years, while they are still cheap to resolve.

### Three common failure shapes

**"Wants it" but doesn't "Get it"** — classic enthusiastic-but-confused hire. Bought in, working hard, but doesn't have the intuitive feel. Wrong seat. Fix is rarely training; it is moving them.

**"Gets it" but "Doesn't want it"** — the painful pattern of someone clearly capable but no longer wanting to do the work. Shows up, delivers competent work, slowly disengages. Wrong seat. Find a seat they actually want, or part ways.

**"Gets it and wants it" but lacks Capacity** — hardest case because everyone is on the same side. They understand, want it, are trying — but cannot deliver the five major roles at the required level. Still wrong seat. The compassionate move is acknowledging the gap and finding a seat where capacity matches demands.

### How GWC interacts with the People Analyzer™

The People Analyzer™ grid combines GWC with Core Values scoring on a single page. Core Values columns use `+ / +/- / -`. GWC columns use `Y / N` only — NO `+/-` allowed in GWC. Any tool that lets a leader put "mostly" in a GWC column has broken the discipline at the form level.

## Key Takeaway
Three binary questions: Get it / Want it / Capacity. All three must be Y. One N = wrong seat (not necessarily wrong person — usually a different seat). NO "mostly," NO `+/-`. Persuading or over-paying someone into wanting it backfires within months.

## When to Apply
- Step 3.2: as soon as the Accountability Chart™ structure exists and names start being placed
- Every hiring decision before an offer goes out
- Every quarterly review cycle for every person
- Before promoting anyone — score against the new seat
- Whenever a leader has a recurring uneasy feeling about a team member's fit
