## The Issues Solving Track™ (IDS) — EOS™ (Gino Wickman)

The Issues Solving Track™ (IDS™) is the discipline that runs inside the 60-minute issue-resolution segment of every L10™. It addresses the most expensive failure mode of leadership meetings: long swirling conversations that neither identify the real problem nor produce a clear next action. IDS replaces that pattern with three sequential steps — Identify, Discuss, Solve.

### Identify — find the root issue

The first and longest step. The central insight: the stated problem is almost never the real problem. What surfaces on the Issues List is usually a symptom. Chasing the symptom produces a fix that holds for a week before the same problem returns wearing a different shirt.

Illustrative patterns:
- A recurring customer complaint about turnaround time may actually be a wrong-seat issue at the GWC™ level
- A sales pipeline problem may actually be a marketing-message problem two steps upstream
- A personality clash between two leaders may actually be an ambiguous accountability boundary on the Accountability Chart™

Before peeling, the issue raiser states which of three types it is:
1. **A genuine problem that needs solving** — work it through to action
2. **Information that needs to be communicated and acknowledged** — no action, just confirm shared understanding
3. **An idea/opportunity needing feedback or green light** — owner asks for go/no-go

Naming the type up front prevents the team from chasing a solution when alignment is needed, or chasing alignment when a yes/no is needed.

### Discuss — every voice once

Each participant gets one full turn to say what they think — and only one. Repeating a position a second time is not discussion; it is politicking. When the same points start cycling, the issue is ready for Solve.

Wickman's standard: participants must "fight for the greater good" — argue for what is right for the whole business, not for their own department. A leader arguing against a decision because it would cost their function headcount, even when it is clearly the right call for the company, is failing the standard.

**Tangent handling:** When someone raises an adjacent topic that pulls the team away, anyone in the room can call out the drift. The tangent is either a real issue worth working (gets written onto the Issues List, picked up later in priority order) or it isn't (dropped, return to the current issue).

### Solve — produce action

A solved issue produces one of three outputs:
- **Action required** — someone owns a concrete next step, reports done/not-done at next L10™ (most common)
- **Alignment or awareness only** — no action, but team confirms shared understanding out loud
- **More research needed** — owner gathers facts, brings them back as a fresh issue

When solved, someone states the resolution out loud in one sentence. Team agrees (issue closed) or someone says "that's not what I heard" (discussion reopens — a feature, catches hidden disagreement before it becomes next week's issue).

### Top-three-first ordering

Do NOT start at the top of the Issues List. Pick the top three by importance (often not the top three on the page) and work them strictly one at a time in priority order. Two reasons: the most important issue is sometimes near the bottom (issues land in the order raised, not severity). Solving the most important issue often dissolves several lower-priority ones because they were symptoms of the same root cause.

### Three coexisting Issues Lists

- **V/TO™ long-term Issues List** — strategic items not urgent this week. Worked at Quarterly Planning.
- **Weekly leadership Issues List** — strategic-altitude items. Worked at the L10.
- **Departmental Issues Lists** — operational items inside a function. Worked at each department's own weekly meeting.

The leadership team should push department-level issues down rather than solving them at the L10.

## Key Takeaway
Three steps in order: Identify (find the root, name the type), Discuss (each voice once, fight for greater good), Solve (action / alignment / research). Top-three-by-importance, not top-three-by-position. Tangents go onto the list, not chased in place.

## When to Apply
- The 60-minute IDS segment of every weekly L10™
- The issues block of every Quarterly Planning Session
- Day Two of every Annual Planning Session
- Every departmental weekly meeting once cascaded

Concepts attributed to Gino Wickman, *Traction™*.
