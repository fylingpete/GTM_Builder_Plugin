## The Visionary and the Integrator — EOS™ (Gino Wickman)

At the top of the Accountability Chart™ in EOS™, Wickman draws a distinction almost no traditional org chart captures: the work of running a growing business is naturally divided between two very different kinds of seats — the Visionary and the Integrator. Roughly half of companies have both seats filled by two different people; the other half have only an Integrator. Either configuration can work. What does not work is forcing one person to be both, or pretending that a single "CEO" box resolves the tension.

The two roles call for almost opposite strengths, and most people great at one are mediocre at the other.

### The Integrator role

The seat that holds the front-line functions together. Where each function head is responsible for a single discipline (sales, operations, finance), the Integrator is responsible for the seams between disciplines. Friction naturally appears between functions as each pushes its own agenda; the Integrator turns that friction into forward momentum rather than letting it become political drag.

Concretely, the Integrator:
- Owns day-to-day execution of the business
- Holds P&L accountability
- Owns the overall business plan
- Removes obstacles so function heads can do their jobs
- Resolves cross-functional disputes the function heads can't resolve among themselves

Wickman uses "Integrator" instead of any standard title because titles vary wildly (CEO, President, COO, MD, GM) while the functional role is the same. Integrator is a *role definition*, not a label on a business card.

**The Integrator role is required.** Always exactly one. Never zero, never two sharing.

### The Visionary role

The seat that appears above the Integrator when a company has someone whose strengths genuinely fit the profile — almost always a founder, owner, or co-owner. The Visionary profile mirrors the Integrator's:

- Highly creative and idea-generative (many new ideas a week, most not great, some that drive the company forward)
- Strong on big, ambiguous, ugly problems nobody else wants to touch
- Weak on tactical follow-through and operational discipline
- Emotionally attuned to the company's culture
- Holder of the company's most important external relationships — major clients, key partners, brand voice in public

**The Visionary seat is optional.** About half of companies have one. Not having a Visionary is not a defect. The Integrator alone can sit at the top when there is no distinct visionary profile to fill, and many healthy companies operate that way.

### Why the partnership works

The two seats work because their strengths are complementary and almost non-overlapping. Visionary supplies *lust*: new ideas, strategic vision, energy, willingness to chase the big opportunity. Integrator supplies *discipline*: focus, prioritization, follow-through, the ability to say "not this quarter" to the Visionary's seventh great idea of the week.

Visionary energy without Integrator discipline → company that pivots constantly and ships nothing. Integrator discipline without Visionary energy → company that runs beautifully but slowly atrophies because nobody is pulling it toward something bigger.

**Lust + discipline = traction.**

### The dotted-line convention

The line connecting the Visionary box to the Integrator box is rendered differently from the lines connecting the Integrator to function heads — typically dotted or visually lighter. The connectors below it are solid.

This is deliberate. The Integrator does NOT "report to" the Visionary the way a function head reports to the Integrator. The two seats are peers in a partnership. Templates that flatten this distinction will cause confusion the first time Integrator and Visionary disagree on something operational.

### Two common failure patterns at the top

**Visionary-only with no Integrator.** A founder unmistakably a Visionary but with no Integrator partner. Symptoms: many great ideas in the air, few of them shipped; decisions made then quietly reversed; function heads with no clear escalation point for cross-functional conflict; team exhausted by direction changes.

The fix is rarely "the Visionary becomes more disciplined." Visionaries are not built that way. The fix is to find or hire an Integrator and genuinely hand over the keys to day-to-day execution. One of the hardest founder transitions because it feels like losing control. In practice the Visionary gains leverage — their ideas finally get executed.

**Integrator promoted out of fit.** The opposite: a high-performing Integrator "promoted" to Visionary because the company assumed it's the next step up. The promoted person now sits in a seat where the work is brainstorming, big relationships, culture — none of which are their strengths. They become quietly unhappy and the company loses its best operator.

Integrator and Visionary are not steps on a ladder. They are different jobs.

## Key Takeaway
Exactly one Integrator (required). Visionary optional, drawn above with a dotted line. Complementary strengths, not a reporting hierarchy. Combining both into a "CEO" box is the most common top-of-chart failure. Promoting an Integrator into a Visionary seat as a reward loses the best operator.

## When to Apply
- Step 3.2 when first drawing the Accountability Chart™
- When the founder is exhausted and the company has stalled — diagnose whether the Integrator seat is missing
- When an Integrator is considered for "promotion" — challenge whether the new seat fits their strengths
- During quarterly reviews when cross-functional decisions keep bottlenecking at the top
