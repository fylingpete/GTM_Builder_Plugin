## The Accountability Chart™ — EOS™ (Gino Wickman)

The Accountability Chart™ is the first tool installed by EOS™ and the structural spine that every other tool hangs off of. At a glance it looks like a conventional org chart — but the question it answers is fundamentally different. A traditional org chart asks "who reports to whom." The Accountability Chart asks "what are the essential functions this business needs to run, and who owns each function." Function before person — that ordering is the entire point.

### Function before name — the discipline

The chart is built in a strict sequence: draw the whole structure first (all seats, all five major roles per seat, all the connecting lines) before any name is placed. Names only get added once the structure is finalized.

Starting with names is the most common failure mode. The moment you place names, you start rationalizing the structure around the people you already have. A weak seat gets preserved because someone you like sits in it. Two seats merge because one person currently does both. A function gets skipped because nobody obvious could run it. These compromises look reasonable in the moment and lock in the exact problems the chart should fix.

### The vertical layers

- **Visionary seat** (optional, ~50% of companies have one) sits at the top, drawn with a dashed/lighter line to mark it as a partnership, not command-and-control.
- **Integrator seat** sits directly below. Required. Always exactly one — two names in the Integrator box is a structural failure, not a compromise.
- **Front-line major functions** below the Integrator. Minimum three: Sales/Marketing, Operations, Finance/Administration. Sweet spot: 3-7 front-line functions. Beyond ten is a warning sign.
- Sub-functions can expand downward from any front-line function as the company grows.

### The 5-roles-per-seat rule

Every seat — Visionary, Integrator, front-line, sub-function — is defined by exactly five major roles. Not three, not seven. Five major accountabilities at the level of "what owning this seat means," not the level of "individual task."

For a function head, the five roles typically include: ownership of the core process the function runs, leadership of the function's team (LMA™ — Lead, Manage, Accountability), responsibility for the function's measurable outcomes, ownership of a key external relationship, and cultural/values stewardship within the function. The exact wording varies; the cardinality and the altitude are fixed.

### LMA — non-delegable leadership

Anyone with direct reports inherits LMA™ — Lead, Manage, and Accountability. Lead = forward-looking clarity of expectations. Manage = day-to-day cadence and unblocking. Accountability = willingness to hold people to commitments and have hard conversations. A manager unwilling to enforce accountability is failing the "A" — and that failure cascades down their chain of the chart.

### Versioned, living artifact

The Accountability Chart is not a one-time deliverable. The structure evolves substantially as the business grows — new functions split out, sub-tiers appear, the leadership team grows. A healthy implementation revisits the chart roughly every 90 days.

### Integration with the rest of EOS

- **Leadership team** = the Visionary (if any) + Integrator + front-line function seats. This is the group at the weekly L10™.
- **Rock owners** must be seats on the chart.
- **Scorecard™ numbers** are owned by seats.
- **Core process ownership** maps processes onto seats.

If the chart is wrong, every downstream tool inherits the problem.

## Key Takeaway
Structure before names. Visionary (optional) + exactly one Integrator + 3-7 front-line functions. Every seat has five major roles. Revisit every ~90 days.

## When to Apply
- Step 3.2 kickoff — the FIRST EOS™ tool installed, before any other
- Whenever the leadership team grows by more than one person
- Whenever a function head leaves and the team is tempted to redistribute the seat informally
- Quarterly review: walk the chart and confirm it describes how the business actually runs
- Annual planning: rebuild from scratch if the business has grown >50% in the year
