## The 10-Year Target™ — EOS™ (Gino Wickman)

The 10-Year Target™ is the answer to question 3 on the V/TO™ and the top of the Vision-to-Traction cascade. It is the long-range destination that everything below has to ladder up to: the 3-Year Picture™ describes the journey's midpoint, the 1-Year Plan describes 12-month milestones, and Quarterly Rocks™ describe the specific 90-day moves. If the 10-Year Target is vague or missing, the entire cascade loses its anchor.

### What the 10-Year Target is

A single, specific, measurable, ambitious goal roughly ten years out. Required properties:

- **One entry, not a list.** Not three, not a portfolio. The discipline of choosing one is the entire point.
- **Specific.** Specific enough that a reader can tell whether the company has hit it without follow-up questions. In practice almost always means a concrete number.
- **Measurable.** Observable. Someone can look at the business in ten years and produce an unambiguous "yes, we hit it" or "no, we did not."
- **Inspiring.** Big enough to stretch — not "what we'd hit anyway if we kept doing what we're doing."
- **Dated.** Tied to a real calendar horizon — a specific year, not "eventually."

### Why one entry, not a list

The temptation in a planning session is to name several long-range goals — revenue, geographic expansion, product, headcount, industry leadership. A list is not a target; it is a wish list. A wish list does not focus the organization the way a single target does.

If there are five targets, there is no destination — there are five competing ones, and the organization splinters attention. If there is one target, every strategy conversation has the same anchor.

The elements the team wanted as additional targets belong on the 3-Year Picture instead, where multiple bullets are appropriate.

### Why 10 years specifically

- **5 years is too close.** Most teams can imagine the company 5 years out as roughly an extrapolation of today. Doesn't stretch imagination enough.
- **25 years is too far.** Leadership team unlikely to all still be there. Industry context unpredictable. Stops feeling like a real commitment.
- **10 years is the sweet spot.** Long enough to require imagining a fundamentally different company; short enough that today's leadership team can credibly say they'll be around to see it happen.

Some teams pick 5 or 20 depending on industry — accepted with a justified reason.

### Top of the Vision-to-Traction cascade

```
10-Year Target → 3-Year Picture → 1-Year Plan → Quarterly Rocks
```

Each layer is derivable from the layer above. A team that has done the cascade properly can read a Rock and trace it upward through a 1-Year goal, into a 3-Year Picture bullet, and to the 10-Year Target. If a Rock cannot be traced upward, either the Rock doesn't belong or the cascade has a gap.

### Rolling the target down

Once the company is within ~3 years of the dated horizon, the long-range target is "rolled down" into the 3-Year Picture and a fresh 10-Year Target is set. A target 3 years out is no longer strategic — it's tactical. The strategic horizon should always be ~a decade ahead.

### Tests

- **30-second test:** could a new hire understand it in 30 seconds and explain it to a friend at dinner?
- **Ambiguity test:** would two people who know the company reach the same answer to "did we hit it?"
- **Number test:** almost every good 10-Year Target has a number — revenue, customers, markets, units, valuation, headcount.
- **Date test:** a specific year, not "eventually" or "in the long run."
- **Stretch test:** reachable but not certain. "We'll definitely hit it" = too modest. "No plausible path" = too ambitious.

## Key Takeaway
One specific, measurable, dated, ambitious goal — not a list. Top of the cascade. Reachable but not certain. Rolls down into the 3-Year Picture when the company gets within 3 years of it.

## When to Apply
- Step 3.2 V/TO™ session, as answer to question 3 of 8
- Annual Planning Session — confirm the target still pulls the team forward, or roll it down if reaching it
- Whenever the company has clearly outgrown the current target
- Whenever the leadership team disagrees about long-range direction (signal the existing target is too vague)
