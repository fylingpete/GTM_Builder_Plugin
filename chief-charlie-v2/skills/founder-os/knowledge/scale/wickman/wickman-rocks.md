## Rocks™ — EOS™ (Gino Wickman)

Rocks™ are the second EOS™ tool installed after the Accountability Chart™. They convert fresh clarity of ownership into 90 days of concrete execution. A Rock is a quarterly priority — not a task, not an open-ended initiative, not a vague aspiration. The discipline is deliberately narrowing: each leader gets only 3-7 Rocks per quarter, and the company itself gets only 3-7 company-level Rocks. When everything is called a priority, nothing actually is.

### What a Rock is (and is not)

A Rock is a quarterly priority with a clear destination, a single owner, and a binary scoring rule at quarter-end. It is not the work itself — it is the outcome the work is supposed to produce. It is not a task (tasks are 7-day items) nor a KPI (KPIs are recurring weekly numbers on the Scorecard™). A Rock is the kind of thing a leader can answer at quarter-end with a clean yes or a clean no.

### The 3-7 rule across tiers

- **Company Rocks per quarter:** 3-7, with the lower end strongly preferred
- **Individual Rocks per leadership team member:** 3-7 total, *inclusive* of company Rocks they personally own (not 3-7 on top)
- **Rocks per non-leadership employee:** 1-3

The principle: focus produces traction; overload produces drift. A leader carrying ten Rocks completes none well. A frontline employee carrying five Rocks loses sight of the day job.

### The SMA criteria

Every Rock must clear three filters before going onto the Rock Sheet:

- **Specific** — destination describable in one plain sentence
- **Measurable** — concrete artifact or number attached
- **Attainable** — plausibly fits into 90 days alongside the owner's day job

At quarter-end the Rock is scored binary: done or not done. No partial credit. The harshness exists because any softer rule lets teams rationalize their way into believing they performed better than they actually did, and the integrity of the discipline collapses within a couple of quarters.

### The single-owner rule

Each Rock has exactly one named owner. Not two co-owners. Not a team. Not a department. When two leaders share ownership, each assumes the other will pick up the ball when things get hard, and the work stalls in the gap. A single named owner closes the gap.

### The 8-step Rock-setting process

Run by the leadership team in a working session at each Quarterly Planning Session:

1. **Brainstorm everything** (typical first pass: 10-20 candidates)
2. **Narrow by keep / kill / combine** until reduced to 3-7 company Rocks
3. **Set due date + sharpen** each surviving Rock to unambiguously SMA
4. **Assign exactly one owner** to each company Rock
5. **Set individual Rocks** — each leader carries forward any company Rocks they own, then adds priorities until they reach 3-7 total
6. **Build the Rock Sheet** — single landscape document with company Rocks at top + each leader's individual Rocks below
7. **Share with the organization** — company Rocks presented at a state-of-the-company meeting capped at 45 min
8. **Cascade to departments** — each department runs the same process, ending with every employee holding 1-3 Rocks

### Rock Sheet — one artifact, not two

A structurally important detail: the Rock Sheet is a single landscape document. Company Rocks at top, individual Rocks below — same page. This enables the Rock Review segment of the weekly L10™ to walk the sheet top-to-bottom in 5 minutes. Two separate files break the cadence.

### First-quarter calibration

Wickman is explicit that first-time Rock-setters complete around half their Rocks. Calibration improves over 2-3 quarters. That's normal, not a sign the tool is broken.

## Key Takeaway
3-7 Rocks per person per quarter. SMA criteria. Exactly one named owner. Binary scoring at quarter-end. Single landscape Rock Sheet for company + individual Rocks. Cascade to departments.

## When to Apply
- Immediately after the Accountability Chart™ is in place (Step 3.2 setup)
- Every Quarterly Planning Session as the centerpiece exercise
- After the V/TO™ is built — run a Rock Alignment Check to ensure existing Rocks trace to the 1-Year Plan
