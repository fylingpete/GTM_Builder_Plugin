## The Three-Step Process Documenter™ — EOS™ (Gino Wickman)

The Three-Step Process Documenter™ is how EOS™ carries out the Process Component — turning the Core Processes from implicit habit into explicit, owned, enforced systems. Named for its three stages: Identify, Document, Compile. The order is fixed; skipping or collapsing any stage produces a predictable failure mode.

Most companies already run on repeatable patterns — but those patterns live inside individual heads and drift quietly over time. The Documenter replaces "ask someone who remembers" with "look at the process," which is the precondition for scaling without losing the quality that made it work.

### Stage 1 — Identify

A single leadership team conversation, typically ≥1 hour, whose entire goal is 100% agreement on the list of core processes and their names. No documentation happens until this list is locked.

Rules:
- **Whole leadership team in the room.** Not delegated, not a sub-committee, not email.
- **Goal is consensus on count and names** — not general agreement, full agreement. A single leader objecting to a name or boundary means the conversation continues.
- **No documentation starts until the list is locked.** Teams that start documenting before identification produce overlapping, inconsistently-named, gappy output that has to be reorganized later.

Skipping this stage is the single most common reason process efforts fail.

### Stage 2 — Document each process

Once the list is locked, each core process is documented by **its owner** — whoever owns the seat that runs the process. Ownership follows the Accountability Chart™.

- **Owners, not temps, do the work.** Not delegated to an intern or consultant. The seat owner knows how the work actually happens and will be enforcing the process afterward.
- **The Integrator owns the overall project** — keeps the effort on track, enforces format, ensures every process gets written within the target timeline.
- **Documentation is not a one-week sprint.** Most companies need 6-12 months to document the full set.

### Stage 3 — Compile and distribute

Individual documents are bundled into a single package (binder, intranet folder, wiki, shared drive — medium does not matter, existence does). Process names become the table of contents. Package is distributed to everyone who touches the processes, and the leadership team trains the organization on the new documents.

From compile-and-distribute onward, processes are enforced. "The way we do it" becomes a real constraint, not a suggestion. Deviations surface as issues in the weekly L10™ and get worked through IDS™. The Scorecard™ measures whether processes are being followed.

Teams that document but never compile usually discover, a year later, that the documents exist on a shared drive nobody has opened.

### The 20/80 rule

The central discipline: document the 20% of content that drives 80% of the results, not the 100% that drives theoretical complete coverage. Teams that try to document everything produce binders nobody reads, and binders nobody reads enforce nothing.

**Sweet spot: 2-10 pages per process.** Under 2 pages = under-documented. Over 10 = over-documented. Operations processes sit on the longer end; people/money flows on the shorter end.

**Over-documentation symptoms:** 20+ page documents, keystroke-level instructions, language trying to eliminate human judgment, sub-procedures embedded inline instead of linked.

**Under-documentation symptoms:** title-list-only, single-page summaries missing sub-steps, missing who-does-what, missing the trigger (what starts/ends the process).

### Structural format

Every core process document follows the same format:
- **Numbered major steps** (typically 3-10 per process)
- **Bullet sub-steps under each major step** (typically a handful)

NOT flowcharts, NOT swimlane diagrams, NOT procedural code, NOT free-form narrative, NOT keystroke-level. Boring formats are easy to produce, read, maintain, and enforce.

### A recurring side effect — surfacing dead weight

One useful side effect of documenting: discovering steps that exist only for historical reasons. Traditions without a live reason should be questioned, not preserved. Documentation is the moment to ask, for each step, whether it is actually doing anything today. Teams that take this seriously remove several steps per process during Document.

### Naming discipline carries through

The name the leadership team agreed on in Stage 1 is used in Stage 2 (document title), in Stage 3 (table of contents), and in every subsequent conversation. No departmental variants, no legacy aliases, no "we call it X here and Y over there." Naming discipline preserves enforceability.

## Key Takeaway
Three stages in order: Identify (full team consensus on count + names), Document (owners only, 2-10 pages, numbered steps + bullet sub-steps, 20/80 level), Compile (bundle + distribute + train + enforce). Skipping Stage 1 produces incoherent output. Skipping Stage 3 produces shelfware.

## When to Apply
- Step 3.2 kickoff after foundational tools are in place
- Whenever a new core process is added — run mini-Documenter (Identify with leadership, Document by owner, Compile into package, train)
- Whenever an existing process has drifted materially — rerun Stage 2 for that process only
- Annual Planning Session — review the compiled package, flag processes needing updates
