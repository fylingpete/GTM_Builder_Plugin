## The Six Key Components — EOS™ (Gino Wickman)

The Entrepreneurial Operating System™ (EOS™) frames every growing business as a machine made of six interlocking components. The point of the framing is diagnostic: it gives a leadership team a shared vocabulary for where the business is healthy and where it is leaking energy. Weakness in any single component quietly undermines the other five — and trying to compensate by doubling down on a strength does not work.

### The six components

- **Vision** — getting every person on the same page about where the business is going and how it will get there. Primary tool: the Vision/Traction Organizer™ (V/TO™), a deliberately two-page document.
- **People** — putting the right people in the right seats. Primary tools: the Accountability Chart™ (function-first seat design), the People Analyzer™ (grid that scores Core Values + GWC™), and the GWC™ filter (Get it / Want it / Capacity).
- **Data** — running the business on a small set of weekly leading indicators rather than trailing financials. Primary tool: the Scorecard™, a rolling 13-week grid of 5-15 numbers with one named owner each.
- **Issues** — surfacing problems honestly and resolving them at root cause. Primary tool: a continuously maintained Issues List worked through Issues Solving Track™ (IDS™ — Identify, Discuss, Solve).
- **Process** — identifying the 5-15 core workflows the business actually runs on and documenting each at the 20/80 level. Primary tool: the Three-Step Process Documenter™.
- **Traction** — discipline and accountability in execution. Primary tools: quarterly Rocks™ (3-7 priorities per quarter, single owner, binary done/not-done) and the Meeting Pulse™ (weekly Level 10™ + quarterly + annual cadence).

### Why all six matter together

The components are not independent — they structurally depend on each other:

- A V/TO without People work in place becomes an aspirational document nobody owns.
- A Scorecard without an Accountability Chart produces numbers with no real owner.
- Rocks without a Meeting Pulse drift by week four.
- Process documents without Traction discipline get filed and forgotten.
- Issues without Vision can be solved cleanly while still moving the business in the wrong direction.

The system runs at the speed of its weakest part. This is the central claim — and the most common scaling failure mode is investing heavily in two strong components (usually Vision and Traction) while leaving People, Data, Issues, or Process underbuilt.

### Measuring with the Organizational Checkup™

The leadership team measures the health of each component on the Organizational Checkup™ — twenty statements (roughly three or four per component) scored on a five-point scale.

- Target state: ≥80% overall.
- Any component below 80% signals that it needs focused work in the next quarter.
- Run at least twice per year (typically mid-year and at the annual planning session) to track progress over time.
- A component score moving backwards quarter-over-quarter is itself an issue that goes onto the long-term Issues List.

### How this maps onto Phase 10

In Skok's Phase 10 (Scale the Organization), all six components have to be installed to support a company crossing ~$10M ARR / ~50 employees. The mistake at this stage is to install Vision and Traction (the most visible ones) and assume the rest will follow. They will not. The discipline of the six-component framing is making sure People, Data, Issues, and Process get the same attention as the headline two.

## Key Takeaway
Six components, all required, none substitutable. The business runs at the speed of its weakest one. Measure health twice a year on the Organizational Checkup™; rebuild any component scoring below 80%.

## When to Apply
- First-time leadership team adopting EOS™ for organizational scaling (Step 3.2)
- Annual Organizational Checkup™ during the annual planning session
- Mid-year diagnostic when leadership feels stuck without being able to name what's wrong
- Onboarding any new leadership team member to give them shared vocabulary
