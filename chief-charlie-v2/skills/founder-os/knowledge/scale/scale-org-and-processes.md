# Scale the organization & processes

Step 3.2 begins when Phase 9 has produced enough volume that the founder
personally cannot continue to make every decision. Slack pings at
midnight, single-thread approvals stacking up, hiring rounds that fail
to fill seats, a leadership team that defers cross-functional calls
rather than making them. Skok's framing: the company is no longer a
startup. It is a scaling organization with its own management problem,
and the founder's prior superpower (jumping in to fix things personally)
has become the bottleneck capping the company's growth.

The install that resolves this is EOS™ (Gino Wickman) — Accountability
Chart™, Rocks™, Meeting Pulse™ with L10™, Scorecard™, V/TO™, and the
Three-Step Process Documenter™. Skok diagnoses the Phase 10 transition;
EOS™ is the toolkit that actually executes it. If 3.1 was "more fuel
into a proven engine," 3.2 is "rebuild the engine so it doesn't shake
itself apart at speed."

## The founder's job has four levers — and only four

Skok's framing of the new founder seat in Phase 10: everything else
delegates. The four levers the founder still owns are People (who is in
which seat), Vision (where are we going), Capital (how cash is
allocated), and Bar (what "good enough" means here). Day-to-day
operations, individual decisions, cross-functional firefighting — none
of those belong on the founder's calendar anymore. The hardest part is
not naming the four levers; it is genuinely letting go of everything
else.

The Visionary seat in EOS™ formalizes this. About half of companies
have a Visionary at the top of the Accountability Chart™ (paired with
the Integrator); the other half don't and operate fine with the
Integrator alone. Either configuration works. What does not work is
forcing one person to be both at the same time.

## The Six Key Components™ — the diagnostic frame

Wickman frames every growing business as a machine of six interlocking
components: **Vision, People, Data, Issues, Process, Traction**.
The point of the framing is diagnostic — it gives a leadership team
a shared vocabulary for where the business is healthy and where it is
leaking energy. The system runs at the speed of its weakest component.
A strong Vision cannot compensate for weak People work. A strong
Scorecard cannot compensate for an Issues List that never gets worked.

The leadership team measures each component twice a year on the
Organizational Checkup™ — twenty statements, five-point scale, target
≥ 80% overall. Any component below 80 signals focused work in the
next quarter. A component score moving backwards quarter-over-quarter
becomes itself a long-term issue. The Organizational Checkup™ is one
of the three Scale cross-step KPIs persisted in `path_state.cross_kpis`
as the Leadership-Health-Score; running it for the first time is a
typical baseline Rock in 3.1, and it becomes a recurring quarterly
read here in 3.2.

The most common scaling failure mode at this step: install the two most
visible components (Vision and Traction) heavily, and assume People,
Data, Issues, and Process will follow. They will not.

## The Accountability Chart™ — structure before names

The Accountability Chart™ is the first EOS™ tool installed and the
structural spine that every other tool hangs off of. At a glance it
looks like a conventional org chart, but the question it answers is
different. A traditional org chart asks who reports to whom. The
Accountability Chart™ asks what the essential functions are and who
owns each function. Function before person — that ordering is the
entire discipline.

The chart is built in a strict sequence: draw the whole structure
(every seat, every connector, every seat's five major roles) before
any name is placed. Starting with names is the most common failure
mode — the moment you place names, you start rationalizing the
structure around the people you already have. A weak seat gets
preserved, two seats merge, a function gets skipped — and the chart
locks in the exact problems it was supposed to fix.

**Structure layers:**

- **Visionary** (optional, ~50% of companies have one), drawn with a
  dotted/lighter line to mark it as a partnership rather than a
  reporting relationship.
- **Integrator** (required, exactly one). Two names in the Integrator
  box is a structural failure, not a compromise.
- **Front-line major functions** below the Integrator: minimum three
  (Sales/Marketing, Operations, Finance/Administration); sweet spot
  3-7; beyond ten is a warning sign.
- **Sub-functions** can expand downward as the company grows.

Every seat — Visionary, Integrator, front-line, sub-function — is
defined by exactly five major roles. Not three, not seven. Five
accountabilities at the altitude of "what owning this seat means,"
not the altitude of "individual task."

**Visionary + Integrator** is a partnership of complementary
strengths, not a hierarchy. Visionary supplies lust (ideas, energy,
big external relationships, willingness to chase the long bet);
Integrator supplies discipline (focus, prioritization, follow-
through, the ability to say "not this quarter" to the Visionary's
seventh great idea of the week). Lust + discipline = traction.
Visionary alone produces a company that pivots constantly and ships
nothing. Integrator alone produces a company that runs beautifully
but slowly atrophies.

The Accountability Chart™ is not a one-time deliverable. Healthy
implementations revisit it roughly every 90 days, and rebuild from
scratch annually if the business has grown more than 50% in the year.

## Right Person, Right Seat — Core Values + GWC™

Once the chart's structure is locked, names go in. EOS™ uses two
filters, scored on a single grid called the People Analyzer™:

**Right Person — Core Values.** 3-7 behaviorally-testable values
discovered (not invented) by the leadership team. Each person scored
on a three-symbol scale per value: `+` (lives the value most of the
time), `+/-` (sometimes), `-` (rarely). The `+/-` mark exists
intentionally because Core Values are cultural behaviors observed
over months, not binary attributes.

**Right Seat — GWC™ (Get it / Want it / Capacity).** Three columns,
each scored `Y` or `N` only. **No `+/-` allowed in GWC™ columns.**
Two-out-of-three is not a partial pass — it is a fail. Tolerance for
ambiguity on GWC™ is the single most common reason the rest of the
EOS™ install quietly stops working.

The bar — minimum acceptable score for anyone in the company — is set
once and applied consistently. Wickman's recommended starting bar
for a company with 5 Core Values: 3 `+`, 2 `+/-`, 0 `-`, plus `Y`
on all three of G/W/C. Below-the-bar triggers the Three-Strike Rule
(three structured conversations over ~90 days), not immediate
termination. Most people either rise above the bar within the first
two strikes, or self-select out before the third strike because the
process makes the mismatch obvious to them too.

A `N` on Capacity does not mean the person is lesser. It means this
specific person × this specific seat is wrong today. The right move
is almost always a different seat, not termination.

## The execution heartbeat — Rocks™, Scorecard™, L10™

Step 3.1 introduced Rocks™. Step 3.2 installs the cadence around
them.

**Scorecard™** — 5-15 weekly **leading-indicator** numbers (target
closer to 5), one named owner per row, 13-week rolling grid with
WHO as the leftmost column. Activity-based (weekly qualified
leads, weekly cash, weekly throughput) — not P&L-based. Monthly
revenue does not pass this test; it is the outcome itself, observed
after the fact. Once the V/TO™ exists, each weekly goal derives
from the 1-Year Plan, so hitting every weekly goal delivers the
annual plan by construction.

**Level 10 Meeting™ (L10™)** — 90 minutes weekly, same day, same
time, leadership team only. Seven-item agenda with locked time
allocations: Segue 5 · Scorecard™ review 5 · Rock™ review 5 ·
Headlines 5 · To-Do review 5 · **IDS™ 60** · Conclude 5. Scorecard™
and Rock™ reviews are **report-only** — on-track or off-track, no
discussion. Misses drop to the Issues List for IDS™ later. The
instinct to fix during reporting blows past 90 minutes and the
important issues at the bottom of the list never get reached.
Same day, same time, same agenda — non-negotiable. Once the L10™
starts moving around, it decays out of existence within months.

## The V/TO™ — built after the foundational tools, not before

The counter-intuitive EOS™ sequencing: the Vision/Traction Organizer™
(V/TO™) is installed **fifth**, after the Accountability Chart™,
Rocks™, Meeting Pulse™, and Scorecard™. The natural founder instinct
is to start with vision. Wickman argues against it for practical
reasons.

A vision session run by a team already living inside execution
discipline produces grounded, debated planning — they know what the
business can actually execute because they've been measured against
weekly reality for months. A vision session run without that
discipline produces aspirational fluff that nobody owns.

The V/TO™ is two pages, eight questions, full leadership-team
authorship in a 1-2 day off-site. Page 1 (Vision, slow-changing):
Core Values · Core Focus™ · 10-Year Target™ · Marketing Strategy ·
3-Year Picture™. Page 2 (Traction, fast-changing): 1-Year Plan ·
Quarterly Rocks™ · Long-Term Issues List. Each layer derives from the
layer above; a team that skips layers creates a cascade with gaps.

Solo-authored V/TOs are structurally invalid — the leadership team's
commitment is proportional to how much each leader contributed to
writing it.

## Core Processes™ — the Three-Step Process Documenter™

The Process Component is the one most often skipped. Companies run on
patterns that live inside individual heads and drift quietly over
time. The Three-Step Process Documenter™ converts those patterns into
explicit, owned, enforced systems.

**Stage 1 — Identify.** One leadership-team conversation, full team
present, ≥1 hour. Goal: 100% consensus on the count and names of
the company's core processes (typically 5-15). No documentation
starts until the list is locked. Skipping this stage is the single
most common reason process efforts fail — teams produce overlapping,
inconsistently-named, gappy output that has to be reorganized.

**Stage 2 — Document.** Each core process is documented by its
**owner** (the seat that runs it, per the Accountability Chart™) —
not by an intern, not by a consultant, not by the temp. The owner
knows how the work actually happens and will enforce the process
afterward. Format: numbered major steps (typically 3-10) + bullet
sub-steps. Length: 2-10 pages per process. The 20/80 rule: document
the 20% that drives 80% of the results; teams that try to document
everything produce binders nobody reads.

**Stage 3 — Compile.** Individual documents bundled into one package
(binder, intranet, wiki — medium doesn't matter, existence does).
Distributed. Leadership team trains the organization. From this
point onward, processes are enforced; deviations surface in the
L10™ Issues List. Teams that document but never compile usually
discover, a year later, that the documents exist on a shared drive
nobody has opened.

A useful side effect of documenting: surfacing dead-weight steps that
exist only for historical reasons. Documentation is the moment to
ask, for each step, whether it is actually doing anything today.

## Hiring velocity — the new rate-limiter

In Step 3.1 lead-supply was the dominant rate-limiter. In 3.2 it is
hiring velocity. The math compounds: each pod requires 1 SDR + 1 AE
+ 0.25 CSM; each pod takes ~4 months to hire and ~6 months to ramp.
To double bookings in 12 months, the hiring engine has to be running
12 months ahead of the bookings plan.

Recruiter capacity becomes a real constraint at scale. Founder-led
recruiting fails at this volume — Step 3.2 installs either an
in-house recruiter or a high-trust external partner working from a
written ideal-candidate profile per role. The single-rep test from
3.1 (cohort-of-two hiring per Schatz, so that failures are
diagnosable) carries forward: pairs not individuals, same playbook,
same manager.

Manager threshold: seven reps maximum per manager. The 8th rep
triggers a second manager. A common mistake is promoting the top
AE to manage pod 2 — the AE's coaching skills are unproven and a
high-performing AE seat goes empty. Better is to hire externally
with documented sales-management experience and leave the top AE
producing.

## The first executive hires — spike over seniority

First VP-level hires are the most-leveraged hiring decisions in a
company's history. YC's data: ~50% of first-executive hires fail,
almost always because the founder pattern-matched on resume rather
than on the work the company actually needs done. Four disciplines
push the failure rate down:

- **Hire for the next 18 months, not the next 5 years.** A senior
  VP from a 500-person team installs Big-Co process that breaks a
  30-person startup. Match the team size they're stepping into,
  not the one you hope to have.
- **Hire for the spike.** A great first VP Sales has built a
  playbook from zero, hired and trained the first 3-7 reps
  personally, and produced bookings that hit a number. The spike
  is the work they will repeat.
- **Reference deeply.** Sam Altman's rule: 10+ back-channel
  references, not the candidate's official list. "Would you hire
  this person again?" — hesitation is the answer.
- **90-day plan, written and signed.** Concrete deliverables,
  decisions, metrics, both parties signing before start date. The
  diagnostic window is 90 days, not 9-12 months — a bad exec hire
  delayed costs 12-18 months of organizational momentum.

## Exit criteria — the org-readiness test

Step 3.2 is done when all seven are true:

1. **Accountability Chart™ current and revisited within 90 days.**
   Every seat has one named owner and five major roles. Visionary
   configuration explicit (Visionary + Integrator, or Integrator alone).
2. **Every leadership-team member at or above the bar** on the People
   Analyzer™ (Core Values + GWC™); same bar applied to the next layer.
3. **Meeting Pulse™ running.** L10™ weekly for ≥ 8 consecutive weeks,
   end-of-meeting ratings averaging ≥ 8. Quarterly + Annual Planning
   on the calendar.
4. **Scorecard™ stable** — 5-15 weekly leading indicators with named
   owners, ≥ 8 weeks of data, goals tied to the V/TO™ 1-Year Plan.
5. **V/TO™ built and signed off** in an off-site, with the 10-Year
   Target™ → 3-Year Picture™ → 1-Year Plan → Quarterly Rocks cascade
   derivable end-to-end.
6. **Core Processes™ identified, documented, and compiled.** Stage 1
   list locked by full team consensus; ≥ 80% of processes documented
   at 20/80 level; package distributed and trained out.
7. **Organizational Checkup™ ≥ 70**, with a credible plan to reach
   ≥ 80 within two quarters.

When all seven are true, the leadership team is running the
organization. Steps 3.3 and 3.4 become decisions the team can execute
without re-routing through the founder.

## Anti-patterns

- **Installing Vision + Traction heavily, skipping People + Data +
  Issues + Process.** The system runs at the speed of its weakest
  component. A great V/TO™ with a broken Accountability Chart™
  produces a beautiful document nobody owns.
- **Names before structure on the Accountability Chart™.** Sculpting
  seats around the people currently in them locks in the exact
  problems the chart exists to fix.
- **Two co-Integrators.** Always exactly one. Two means each
  assumes the other will pick up the ball when things get hard,
  and the work stalls in the gap.
- **`+/-` in GWC™.** The form itself has to forbid it; once you
  allow "mostly" on seat fit, the discipline is dead within a quarter.
- **Discussion during Scorecard™ or Rock™ review in the L10™.**
  Blows past 90 minutes; the truly important issues at the bottom
  of the IDS™ list never get reached. Report-only is non-negotiable.
- **V/TO™ built before foundational tools.** Without execution
  discipline already in place, the vision session produces
  aspirational fluff the team doesn't own.
- **Document but never Compile.** Documents exist on a shared
  drive nobody opens. The compile-and-distribute step is what
  turns documentation into enforcement.
- **First executive hired for the next 5 years.** They install
  Big-Co process that breaks the startup. Hire for 18 months.
- **Top AE promoted to manage pod 2.** Coaching skills unproven,
  AE seat empty, both pods underperform.
- **Founder still routing every cross-functional decision.** The
  symptom that 3.2 is incomplete: the Integrator seat exists on
  paper but the founder is functionally still the Integrator.

## Connection to other steps

- **From 3.1:** The proven engine and the pod-replication unit
  arrive here intact. Rocks™ are already running. Step 3.2 adds
  the structural spine, the cadence, and the org that lets the
  engine compound without breaking.
- **To 3.3 (International expansion):** Country-GTM-Hire decisions
  are executed by the new VP Sales / VP International, not the
  founder personally. The Accountability Chart™ acquires a new
  seat (Country/Region GM) per market entered.
- **To 3.4 (Product-line expansion):** The Visionary's Core
  Focus™ ("stay in the cone") and Core Focus™ disciplines are the
  filter that distinguishes a new product line from a shiny-object
  pivot. Adjacent-Audience-Test belongs in 3.4; the discipline that
  enables it belongs here.
- **To Annual Planning:** This is the first step where the Annual
  Planning Session (Scale-only operation) becomes a recurring
  artifact — V/TO™ refresh, 3-Year Picture™ rebuild,
  Organizational Checkup™.
