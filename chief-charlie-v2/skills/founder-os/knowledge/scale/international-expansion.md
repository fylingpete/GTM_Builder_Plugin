# Expand internationally

Step 3.3 begins when the home market is showing maturity signals — pipeline
constant rather than growing, top-of-funnel cost rising, top competitor
operating in the same segment — and the leadership team can choose where
to expand next. Skok's Phase 11 names three valid vectors (geographic,
second product, vertical-depth) and one discipline: **pick one, prove it,
then add a second.** Step 3.3 is the geographic vector. Step 3.4 covers
product-line expansion. Vertical-depth is mostly executed within 3.1 + 3.4
rather than as a step of its own.

International is the most over-romanticized vector. Founders pitch it to
boards as "doubling the market"; they under-model that each new country
is effectively a startup-inside-the-startup with its own PMF risk, its
own hiring, its own regulatory exposure, and its own cash-trough math.
The YC framing applies: international only works when the home playbook
is so dialed that the founders are bored running it. Expansion before
that point exports chaos to a new country instead of growth.

This step is heaviest on Sales, People (the country GM hire), and
Finance (FX, country unit economics, two-currency cash flow). Product
work is mostly localization rather than redesign — the discipline of
3.3 is restraint about what to change versus replicate.

## The replication mindset, not the invention mindset

The single highest-leverage frame at the start of 3.3: international
expansion is replication of a proven motion, not the invention of a new
one. The playbook from the home market — ICP definition, qualifying
script, pricing model, sales cycle, onboarding sequence, customer success
motion — gets carried into the new country *as-is*, then adjusted only
where the new country's reality demands it.

The mirror-image failure is treating expansion as an opportunity to
"finally do it right" — re-invent positioning, rebuild the funnel,
rethink pricing. This produces a country effort that takes 3-4x longer
than replication because the team is solving two problems at once: a new
country AND a new playbook. Replicate first; adjust second.

## Pick the first country by buyer-behavior similarity, not TAM

The biggest TAM is almost never the right first country. Three reasons:

- **Buyer behavior matters more than market size.** A $5B addressable
  market that buys SaaS the way the home market does beats a $50B market
  that buys via 12-month enterprise sales cycles the team has never run.
- **Language and legal proximity matter.** Anglosphere → Anglosphere
  (US → UK → Canada → AU/NZ) is the easiest jump. EU → EU works when
  the home team has German or French speakers on the leadership team.
  Anglosphere → Japan/Korea/China is the hardest jump and rarely the
  right first move.
- **Regulatory friction can kill economics country-by-country.**
  Healthcare, finance, education, public-sector each have country-
  specific certifications. Without budget for compliance work, the
  expansion stalls at month six.

Pick the country with the highest similarity-to-home score, not the
largest absolute market. The countries with the largest TAM (Japan,
Germany, China) are typically reserved for country 3 or country 4,
after the team has built replication muscle with easier expansions.

## The 18-24 month break-even reality

Founders model international as "we'll be cashflow positive in 12
months." YC's data and Skok's portfolio experience both say 18-24. The
breakdown:

- 3-6 months to hire the first country GM
- 3-6 months for the GM to build the team (2-4 first hires)
- 6-9 months of sales-cycle time before bookings ramp meaningfully
- Localization debt running in parallel: legal entity, tax registration,
  payment processing, support hours, content, contracts in local
  language, GDPR/equivalent

Total to first material bookings: ~12 months. Total to country-level
break-even: 18-24 months. Budget cash for the full 24. If the cash
runway can't sustain the country investment for 24 months on top of the
home market's continued spending, the expansion is delayed, not
attempted on a shorter budget.

This is the same cash-trough math from 3.1 applied at country granularity.
Each new country deepens the trough before it shallows it. Modeling
"first country profitable in 12 months" produces a runway gap that has
to be closed by an emergency fundraise on bad terms — the country
expansion's failure mode that founders most consistently underestimate.

## The country GM is the deciding hire

The first hire in any new country is the most important hire of the
expansion. Not the AE, not the marketer — the GM who owns the country's
P&L. The profile:

- 8-15 years experience in the target country
- Ideally has launched a US (or home-market) subsidiary before
- Speaks the language natively or to native-equivalent business standard
- Accepts a high-equity / lower-base comp structure aligned with the
  company's cash position
- Has hired and managed local sales teams in this country before

Hiring this person is 6-9 months of full founder (or VP International)
attention. Cutting corners on the GM hire is the second most-cited
expansion failure mode after picking the wrong first country.

The Accountability Chart™ acquires a new front-line seat (Country/Region
GM) per market entered. The GM reports to the Integrator or to a newly-
created VP International seat when there are ≥ 2 countries. They sit on
the L10™ if the country is material to total ARR (typically > 10%); they
get a quarterly business review with the leadership team if smaller.

## Centralize what scales, localize what doesn't

The third discipline. Get the wrong things on each side of the line and
either scale breaks (over-localized) or the new country never gets
traction (under-localized).

**Centralize:** product, engineering, brand, pricing strategy,
positioning, security/compliance architecture. These compound across
countries and benefit from a single owner.

**Localize:** sales motion (selling cycle, follow-up cadence, demo
content), customer success playbook (hours, language, contract handling),
marketing tone (channel mix, content style, partnerships), legal
documents (terms, DPAs), pricing tactics (not strategy — currency,
discount norms, payment terms).

The most common mis-allocation: centralizing the sales motion (taking
the home playbook verbatim and expecting it to work) or localizing the
product (building per-country features that fragment the codebase). The
first under-localizes; the second over-localizes. Both are common.

## The 3-Year Picture™ anchors the multi-country plan

The V/TO™'s 3-Year Picture™ is the planning artifact that prevents
country-by-country drift. The annual planning session names which
countries the company will have entered by year +3, what each country's
share of ARR will be, what the country GM org will look like, and what
total operating margin the country mix will produce. Concrete future
date, concrete revenue, concrete headcount, ~10-20 "looks like" bullets
describing the multi-country operation.

A 3-Year Picture™ that doesn't enumerate countries forces every annual
planning session to re-debate the expansion. A concrete one cascades
into the 1-Year Plan (which country, which GM, which targets), which
cascades into Quarterly Rocks™ (which milestones in which country).
The cascade from 3.2 makes 3.3 executable; without it, international
becomes a perpetual board discussion.

## Set kill criteria before launch

Before the first country launches, write the kill criteria. Standard
form: "If this country does not produce $X ARR by month 18 (or $Y by
month 24), we close the country and reallocate the spend." Without
written kill criteria, countries linger for years with no clear exit —
draining cash and leadership attention while everybody waits for "the
breakthrough" that doesn't come.

A country that hits kill criteria is closed, not nursed. The discipline
is the same as the channel kill criteria from 3.1: the criterion is set
before the test, and the test produces a binary outcome. Negotiating
the kill criterion downward when the country misses is the failure mode.

Common kill thresholds: < 30% of home-market PPR at 18 months;
> 36-month CAC payback persisting for 2 consecutive quarters; country
operating loss > 1.5× planned through month 18. Adjust to the
company's economics, but write them down.

## Sequencing, not parallelism

Skok's Phase 11 discipline applied to 3.3: prove the first country
contributes ≥ 10% of new ARR for ≥ 2 consecutive quarters before
launching a second. Parallel country launches (the "blanket Europe"
plan) split leadership attention so thinly that no country reaches
escape velocity. The board pressure to "show ambition" makes parallel
expansion tempting; the math makes it expensive.

The right cadence: country 1 launches → country 1 hits the proof
threshold → country 2 launches with a different GM running the same
playbook. Countries 3 and 4 can sometimes overlap (the team has
replication muscle by then); countries 1 and 2 almost never should.

## Exit criteria — the international-readiness test

Step 3.3 is done when all six are true:

1. **First country meets the proof threshold** — contributing ≥ 10% of
   new ARR for ≥ 2 consecutive quarters, with country unit economics
   matching or beating the home market's LTV:CAC and CAC payback.
2. **Country GM seat is fully owned** — on the Accountability Chart™,
   sitting in the appropriate cadence (L10™ if material, QBR otherwise),
   carrying their own Rocks™.
3. **Replication playbook is documented** as a Core Process™ —
   "Launching a new country" — at the 20/80 level (2-10 pages), so
   country 2 + country 3 don't re-derive the work.
4. **Country-level unit economics dashboard exists** with FX-adjusted
   LTV:CAC, CAC payback, and operating margin per country.
5. **Kill criteria written and applied** — at least one country has
   been kept or closed against pre-written kill criteria (not retroactive
   judgment). If no country has hit kill criteria, the criteria still
   exist in writing.
6. **3-Year Picture™ has been refreshed** to reflect the multi-country
   operation, with country-by-country revenue and headcount
   enumerated.

When all six are true, the international vector is producing
compounding returns and the company can choose to add country 2 + 3 (run
the same playbook again) or pivot to 3.4 (product-line expansion as
the next vector).

## Anti-patterns

- **Picking the first country by TAM.** Buyer-behavior similarity is
  the right axis. The largest market is usually country 3 or 4.
- **Treating expansion as invention.** Rebuilding the playbook in
  parallel with launching a country doubles the work and triples the
  failure rate.
- **Hiring AEs before the country GM.** The GM is the most important
  hire; AEs hired before the GM end up either reporting to the wrong
  person or getting fired and rehired by the GM later.
- **No kill criteria, or retroactive kill criteria.** Countries linger
  for years in "promising but not yet" without written thresholds.
- **Modeling 12-month break-even per country.** Reality is 18-24.
  The runway gap from this single mistake is the failure mode that
  forces the bad-terms emergency fundraise.
- **Centralizing the sales motion.** The home-market playbook needs
  local adjustment (cadence, channel mix, contract norms). Replication
  is not verbatim copying.
- **Localizing the product.** Per-country feature branches fragment
  the codebase. Localization is content, language, payment, support —
  not product surface.
- **Parallel multi-country launch (the "blanket Europe" plan).** Splits
  leadership attention so thinly that no country reaches escape
  velocity. Sequential beats parallel.
- **Country GM on home-market comp structure.** Local benchmarks +
  cost of living + equity weighting differ. Pay the country, not the
  home office.

## Connection to other steps

- **From 3.2:** The Accountability Chart™ provides the structural
  template for adding the GM seat. The L10™ + Scorecard™ + V/TO™
  cadence makes multi-country review feasible. Core Processes™ make
  country-launch a replicable artifact rather than a re-invention.
- **From 3.1:** The proven motion is what gets replicated. Pod math
  applies country-by-country — the country GM's first hires form the
  country's first pod.
- **To 3.4 (Product-line expansion):** 3.3 and 3.4 are parallel-okay
  vectors after 3.2-Exit. A company can do them sequentially in either
  order, or run one and skip the other depending on what the home
  market signals show. Don't run both simultaneously.
- **To Annual Planning:** Country mix is a permanent agenda item once
  the first country is live — country count, country share of ARR,
  country GMs in seats.
