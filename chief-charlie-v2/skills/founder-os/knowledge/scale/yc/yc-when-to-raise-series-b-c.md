## When to Raise Growth-Stage Capital — Y Combinator

Growth-stage capital (Series B/C) has the highest leverage and the highest cost. A correctly-timed Series B compresses 3 years of growth into 18 months. A wrongly-timed one dilutes founders permanently with no compensating growth.

YC partners' framing: the question is not "can we raise" — at scaling stage, capital is almost always available. The question is "should we raise, now, for this purpose, on these terms."

### The two preconditions

**(1) Proven unit economics.** All three gates from Skok Phase 8 are green for ≥2 consecutive months:
- LTV:CAC > 3
- CAC payback < 18 months
- NDR > 100% (or clear path to it)

Raising before unit economics are proven means the round funds losses at scale. Every additional dollar acquired customer makes the company's economics worse, not better. Founders who raise without proven economics often raise again 12 months later — at a lower valuation — to fund the previous mistake.

**(2) Credible capital deployment plan.** A written plan for what the cash will fund: hiring plan (specific roles, specific months), sales-and-marketing budget (specific channels, specific ROI assumptions), product investment (specific bets, specific outcomes). Without the plan, raised cash sits in the bank earning 4% while the round dilutes founders 20%.

### How much to raise

The rule of thumb: raise for 24-36 months of runway at the planned spend rate. Less than 24 months puts the next round on the calendar before this round produces results. More than 36 months invites the temptation to over-spend "because we have it."

Aaron Harris's framing: model the company's cash position over the next 36 months at the planned spend. The right raise is the one that lands the company at "should raise the next round from strength, with results, on better terms."

### Pick the right investor, not the highest valuation

At growth stage, the investor's brand, network, and operating expertise matter more than the valuation. A round at $300M from a top-tier growth investor is usually better than $400M from a tier-2 investor.

Reasons: the top-tier investor opens doors (later rounds, M&A buyers, public-company introductions), the operating-partner team helps with executive hires, and the secondary market liquidity at exit favors top-tier-backed companies.

### Terms matter more than headlines

Three terms to watch:
- **Liquidation preference** — 1x non-participating is standard; multiples or participating preferences eat founder/employee equity at exit
- **Anti-dilution** — full-ratchet kills founders in a down round; broad-based weighted average is the standard
- **Board composition** — adding 1 investor board seat per round is normal; adding 2+ shifts company control

Founders who chase headline valuation and accept bad terms often discover in year 5 that the terms — not the cap-table-on-paper — determine who actually owns the company.

### When NOT to raise

- Unit economics are not yet proven (raise a smaller bridge, prove economics, then raise the real round)
- No clear plan for the capital (do annual planning first, then raise to fund the plan)
- The board is pushing for a round because it improves their fund's mark, not because the company needs it
- The terms available are bad and waiting 6 months would meaningfully improve them

## Key Takeaway
Raise growth-stage capital when unit economics are proven AND a credible capital deployment plan exists. Aim for 24-36 months of runway. Pick the right investor over the highest valuation. Terms (liquidation, anti-dilution, board) matter more than the headline.

## When to Apply
- Series A is 12-18 months old and the next round is being considered
- All three Phase 8 unit-economics gates are green
- Board is discussing growth-round timing
- Annual planning includes a fundraising line item
- Strategic acquirers or growth investors are reaching out
