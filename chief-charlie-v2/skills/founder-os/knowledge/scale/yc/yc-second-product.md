## Adding a Second Product — Y Combinator

The second product is the vector founders most often pick for the wrong reasons. The pitch sounds compelling: "We have 5,000 customers, we just need to sell them one more thing." The math says ~80% of second products at scaling-stage SaaS companies fail to reach 20% of total ARR.

YC partners' framing: the second product is a new startup. It needs PMF. The fact that you have 5,000 customers does not give it PMF — it gives it distribution, which is a different thing.

### The three properties of a successful second product

**(1) Same ICP.** The second product sells to the same buyer at the same company. Different ICP = different sales motion = different CSM playbook = different unit economics. At that point you're starting a second startup, not extending the first.

**(2) Same distribution channel.** The first product's installed base or sales motion delivers the second product. If the second product requires a new channel — paid ads for the first product, content for the second — the channel-build costs eat the distribution advantage.

**(3) Treated as a startup-inside-the-startup.** Dedicated team, dedicated PM, dedicated GTM lead, own metrics, own roadmap, own quarterly review. Bolted onto the existing product team's roadmap, the second product gets the leftover capacity and dies.

If any of the three is missing, the second product is unlikely to succeed. If all three are present, the success rate is meaningfully higher than starting a brand-new company — because distribution is the hardest part.

### Customer demand is necessary but not sufficient

"Customers are asking for it" is the signal founders point to most often. It is necessary but not sufficient. Customers ask for many things; most of them, when built, are not used.

Validation pattern: pre-sell the second product to 10 customers at the actual price before building it. If 10 customers won't put money down, the asks are polite, not real.

### The hidden cost: leadership attention

The second product's most expensive line item is not engineering — it's leadership attention. Every hour the CEO spends on the second product is an hour not spent on the core. Pre-launch, the core team feels neglected. Post-launch, if the second product takes off, the core often stagnates.

Mitigation: a dedicated GM for the second product, day one. The CEO is the board member, not the operator.

### When to kill the second product

Set kill criteria pre-launch. The clearest is "no PMF signal at 18 months" — using the same Sean Ellis or cohort-retention test you used for the first product. Without kill criteria, second products linger in "promising but not yet" for years, draining capital and leadership attention.

Paul Graham's framing: kill the second product the moment it stops looking like the company's future. Don't let it become a zombie product that the team can't quite kill but also can't grow.

## Key Takeaway
Most second products fail. The successful ones share three properties: same ICP, same channel, treated as startup-inside-the-startup. Customer asks are necessary but not sufficient — pre-sell to 10 customers. Set kill criteria pre-launch; kill at 18 months without PMF.

## When to Apply
- Core product's growth is flattening
- Customers are asking for an adjacent product
- The board is asking for a "platform story"
- The second product is being scoped for next year's roadmap
- Phase 11 expansion vector decision is being made
