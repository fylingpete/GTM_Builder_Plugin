## Scaling the Startup Organization — Y Combinator

When a startup crosses ~30 employees the failure mode shifts. The bottleneck is no longer "we don't have product-market fit" or "we can't sell." The bottleneck is the founder's calendar. Every decision still flowing through one person caps the company's growth at that person's bandwidth.

The transition is uncomfortable because the founder's prior superpower (jumping in and fixing things) becomes the new bottleneck. YC partners describe this as the moment when the founder must stop being the smartest operator in every room and start being the person who decides who the smartest operators are.

### The three questions every scaling founder must answer

1. **Who reports to whom?** Org chart with explicit accountable seats. No "we'll figure it out" reporting lines.
2. **What does each person own?** One person, one outcome. Co-ownership is no-ownership.
3. **At what cadence do they sync?** Weekly all-hands, monthly business review, quarterly planning, annual offsite. Without explicit cadences, decisions queue at the founder.

A founder who can write these three answers on one page is ready to scale past 30. A founder who can't is the bottleneck regardless of how good the product is.

### Hire executives before you're desperate

Sam Altman: the second-most-common scaling mistake (after not raising enough money) is hiring executives too late. By the time you "need" a VP Engineering, the engineering team is already in chaos. The right time to start the executive search is 6-9 months before you'd say "we need this person."

Hiring desperate produces bad hires. Hiring early — with time to test fit, reference deeply, and let the new exec build their team thoughtfully — produces good ones.

### Process is what you install when heroics stop scaling

Pre-scale companies run on heroics. Whoever cares most, does it. At scale this stops working — there are too many things to care about and too few founders to do them. Process is the replacement.

Michael Seibel's frame: process is the company's memory. It's how a 100-person team operates as if all 100 people remembered every previous decision. Without process, every new hire reinvents the same decision the company made six months ago.

The risk: process can ossify. The discipline is installing the minimum process needed to scale this quarter — not the maximum process you might need next year. Re-evaluate quarterly.

### What founders should be doing at scale

Four things, and only four:
1. **Setting direction** — vision, strategy, priorities for the year
2. **Hiring leaders** — the people who run the company on a day-to-day basis
3. **Allocating capital** — where the money goes, what gets funded, what gets cut
4. **Setting the bar** — what "good enough" means in this company

Everything else delegates. Founders who keep "owning customer support" or "running engineering hiring" at scale are signaling that they haven't completed the transition.

## Key Takeaway
Past ~30 employees, the founder's job changes. The new job is who-reports-to-whom, what-they-own, what-cadence-they-sync. Hire executives 6-9 months before you'd say you need them. Install minimum process, re-evaluate quarterly.

## When to Apply
- Headcount has crossed or is approaching 30 employees
- Decisions visibly queue at the founder's calendar
- New hires repeatedly ask "who decides X?"
- The first VP-level hires are being scoped
- Annual planning is being run for the first time
