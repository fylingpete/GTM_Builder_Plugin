## Hiring Your First Executives — Y Combinator

The first executive hires are the most-leveraged hiring decisions in a startup's history. A great first VP Engineering enables 5 years of engineering execution. A bad one takes 9-18 months to remove, leaves an org in chaos, and costs the company a full hiring cycle plus severance plus opportunity cost.

YC partners' framing: most first-executive hires fail. The failure rate at startups for first-time-at-startup execs is ~50%. The failure mode is almost always the same: the founder pattern-matched on the resume, not on the work.

### Hire for the next 18 months, not the next 5 years

The biggest mistake: hiring the executive you'll need at $100M ARR when you're at $5M ARR. The senior person from Salesforce or Google who's run a 500-person team will not be productive running your 12-person team. They will hire 20 more people and try to install Big-Co process — which breaks the startup.

Hire for the work the company actually needs done in the next 18 months. If the team is 12 today, going to 35 in 18 months, hire someone who's run a 35-person team well — not a 500-person team adequately.

### The "spike" matters more than the seniority

A great first VP Sales has a specific spike: they have personally built a sales playbook from zero, hired and trained the first 3-7 reps, and produced bookings that hit a number. The spike is the work they will repeat for you.

Pattern-matching on "VP at $50M ARR company" without understanding the spike produces an executive who has only managed managers — and your company doesn't need a manager of managers, it needs someone who can still close deals personally.

### Reference deeply or don't hire

Sam Altman's rule: 10+ back-channel references before any first executive hire. Not the official references the candidate provides — actual conversations with people who worked above, beside, and below the candidate at their last two jobs.

The key question to ask references: "Would you hire this person again?" Hesitation is the answer. "Yes, definitely" with specifics is the answer. Anything in between is a no.

### Probationary expectations — 90 days, written down

Write the 90-day plan with the executive before they start. Concrete deliverables, concrete decisions they'll be empowered to make, concrete metrics for success. Both founder and executive sign off on it.

At day 90, run a written review. If the executive isn't on the plan, the conversation happens then — not at month 9 when the org is already disrupted.

### Cost of a bad executive hire

A bad VP hire at month 0 isn't removed until month 9-12 on average. Reasons: founders hate firing executives, the cost feels sunk, the org hopes things will improve. The actual cost of waiting:

- 9 months of subpar execution in the executive's domain
- Hires the executive made who must also be evaluated and often released
- Damaged trust in the executive function (the team has seen what "bad" looks like)
- Severance package, replacement search, ramp-up of the next executive

Net: a bad VP hire costs 12-18 months of organizational momentum. The discipline is fast diagnosis — 90 days, written review, decision.

## Key Takeaway
Hire for the next 18 months, not the next 5 years. Hire for the spike — the specific work you need done — not for the seniority. Reference 10+ people deeply. Write a 90-day plan; review honestly at day 90.

## When to Apply
- First VP-level hires are being scoped
- A previous executive hire has failed and the replacement search is starting
- The founder is uncertain what "good" looks like for the executive role
- The first 90-day plan is being written
- Phase 10 org-design includes new executive seats
