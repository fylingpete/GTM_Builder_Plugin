## International Expansion — Y Combinator

International expansion is the most over-romanticized vector in Phase 11. Founders pitch it to boards as "doubling the market" — and underestimate that each new country is effectively a startup-inside-the-startup with its own PMF risk, hiring, regulatory, and cash-trough math.

The YC partners' framing: international expansion only works when the home-market playbook is so dialed that founders are bored running it. Expansion before that point exports chaos to a new country instead of growth.

### Pick the first country by buyer-behavior similarity, not TAM

The biggest TAM is almost never the right first country. Reasons:

- **Buyer behavior matters more than market size.** A $5B addressable market that buys SaaS the way your home market does beats a $50B market that buys via 12-month enterprise sales cycles when you've never sold that way.
- **Language and legal proximity matter.** Anglosphere → Anglosphere (US → UK → Canada → AU) is easiest. EU → EU works if the company has German/French speakers. Anglosphere → Japan/Korea/China is the hardest jump.
- **Regulatory friction can kill economics.** Healthcare, finance, education each have country-specific certifications. Without budget for compliance, the expansion stalls 6 months in.

Pick the country with the highest similarity-to-home score, not the largest absolute market.

### The first country takes 18-24 months to break even

Founders typically model international as "we'll be cashflow positive in 12 months." The data says 18-24 months. Why:

- 3-6 months to hire the first country GM
- 3-6 months for the GM to build the team (2-4 first hires)
- 6-9 months of sales-cycle time before bookings ramp
- Localization debt (legal, tax, currency, support hours, content)

Budget cash for 24 months. If the cash isn't there, delay the expansion.

### Country GM hire is the deciding factor

The first hire in any new country is the most important hire of the expansion. Not the AE, not the marketer — the GM who owns the country's P&L.

Profile: 8-15 years experience in the country, ideally has launched a US-company subsidiary before, speaks the language natively (or to native-equivalent business standard), and accepts a high-equity / lower-base comp structure aligned with the company. Hiring this person is 6-9 months of full founder attention.

### Centralize what scales, localize what doesn't

Product, engineering, brand, pricing strategy — centralize. Sales motion, customer success, marketing tone, partnerships — localize. The mistake: centralizing the sales motion (the home market's playbook) without adjustment, or localizing the product (per-country features) when standardization scales better.

### Kill criteria — set them before launch

Before the first country launches, write the kill criteria: "If we don't have $X ARR by month 18, we close this country." Without kill criteria, expansions linger for years with no clear exit, draining cash and leadership attention. The discipline: a country that hits kill criteria is closed, not nursed.

## Key Takeaway
International expansion is replicating, not inventing. Pick countries by buyer-behavior similarity to home, not TAM. Budget 24 months to break even. Country GM hire is the deciding factor. Set kill criteria pre-launch.

## When to Apply
- Home market growth is flattening despite Phase 9 spending
- Board is pushing for international expansion
- The first country has been picked but not launched
- Country GM search is being scoped
- Annual planning includes a new-country revenue line
