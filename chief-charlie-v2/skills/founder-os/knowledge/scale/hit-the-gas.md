# Hit the gas & scale

Step 3.1 is the binary moment. The motion is repeatable, unit economics
are proven, retention is positive — and the question is no longer *can*
this work but *how fast.* Skok's framing makes the decision explicit:
until unit economics are proven, every growth dollar is at risk. Once
proven, every dollar held back is forfeited to compounding rivals. There
is no middle setting that doesn't fail in one of those two ways.

The phase that founders most often get wrong on both sides. Press too
early — before the three gates are green — and the cash trough swallows
the company while the team blames external conditions. Press too late
— gates green for three quarters, board still cautious — and a
better-funded competitor compounds past you in a market that tends
toward winner-takes-all. The discipline of 3.1 is converting "we have a
working motion" into "we are aggressively pouring fuel onto it" without
breaking the unit economics that made the fuel safe to pour.

This step is mostly Sales, Marketing, and Finance — not Product, not
People-and-Process. Org redesign and the EOS™ install belong in 3.2.
Here the job is volume: more leads, more reps, more cash deployed
intelligently, and the operating cadence (Quarterly Rocks™) that makes
"more" something a leadership team can actually execute on.

## The three green-light gates

Skok's entry gates for Phase 9 — all three must be green for ≥2
consecutive months before the gas pedal is safe to press:

- **LTV:CAC > 3** — every dollar acquiring a customer returns ≥$3 in
  lifetime gross profit. Below 3, scaling multiplies a leaky bucket.
- **CAC payback < 18 months** — cash recovery window inside standard
  SaaS norms. Payback of 6-7 months means the business is *under*-spending
  and has headroom to be more aggressive, not less.
- **NDR (Net Dollar Retention) > 100%** — existing customers expand
  faster than they churn. Below 100%, every new customer cohort is
  diluted by the prior cohorts melting; growth becomes a treadmill.

The two-consecutive-months rule exists because single-month readings
swing on deal mix. Two months is the minimum to filter noise without
forfeiting another quarter to caution. Three green readings are better;
four is over-waiting.

Hitting the gas with any of the three red is the most expensive class
of mistake in the entire 11-phase roadmap. The cash burn is not the
worst part — the worst part is that you cannot tell, three quarters
in, whether the burn is "Phase 9 normal" (cash trough deepening) or
"the gates were never green" (the business doesn't work at scale).

## The cash trough deepens with growth

GAAP accounting was built for one-time sales and assigns no value to the
recurring revenue stream that hasn't been billed yet. So a healthy SaaS
company spending heavily to acquire customers and recovering that cost
over 12-18 months looks — to GAAP — like a money-losing business. The
faster it grows, the worse it looks.

Skok's SaaS cash-flow trough is the cumulative-cash chart shape that
defines this phase. Two facts founders consistently under-model:

1. **CAC is paid up-front; LTV is paid over years.** Every new customer
   makes the trough *deeper* before it makes it shallower. Faster growth
   = deeper trough.
2. **Doubling growth rate roughly doubles the depth of the trough.**
   A 60% growth plan does not produce a "manageable" trough — it
   produces something close to 2× the trough of a 30% plan.

NetSuite passed through three separate cash-flow troughs as they
invested in things like international expansion. Each new growth
investment dipped them back into significant cumulative losses before
the recurring revenue caught up. HubSpot's Matrix board members spent
the better part of a year freaking out at losses growing *faster* than
revenue. The intuition from non-SaaS businesses says "death spiral."
For a healthy SaaS business at Phase 9, it means growth is accelerating.

The diagnostic that distinguishes the two: unit economics. Specifically
LTV:CAC, CAC payback, and gross margin. If those stay healthy as growth
accelerates, the trough is the price of compounding. If they decay as
growth accelerates, the trough is a sign the business does not work at
scale and the gates were green only at small volume.

## Bookings math, not revenue projections

The right model at scale is bookings, not revenue, because revenue lags
bookings by a quarter or more and the lag hides whether the scaling plan
is on track:

```
Bookings = #Reps × PPR × Capacity-Utilization
```

Where:

- **#Reps** is fully-ramped reps. A rep at month 3 of ramp counts as
  ~0.4, not 1.0. A rep at month 6 counts as ~0.7. Skok's typical
  full-ramp window is 6-9 months for B2B AEs.
- **PPR (Productivity Per Rep)** is the annual bookings a fully-ramped
  rep produces, typically 4-6× their OTE.
- **Capacity-Utilization** is the fraction of available rep capacity
  filled by qualified lead-flow. < 70% means lead supply is the
  bottleneck; hiring more reps in that state wastes payroll. > 100%
  means lead-flow is starving call quality.

The discontinuous-growth implication: a rep hired in January is not
contributing fully until April-July at the earliest. A plan that reads
"hit $X by Q3" without an explicit ramp curve will miss by 30-50% on a
fast-hiring quarter.

ARR is a cumulative sum and lags bookings by quarters. Bookings is the
operating metric; ARR is the reporting metric. Watch bookings monthly;
ARR will catch up.

## Channel layering, not channel switching

Hormozi's Core Four — warm outreach, cold outreach, content, paid ads —
maps the four ways to acquire customers. Phase 3.1 does not pick a new
one; it *layers* the next channels on top of the validated Phase 2.1
channel. Every individual channel has an S-curve and a saturation point:

- **Paid search** exhausts the volume of buyers searching for the
  category. You can outbid competitors, but the audience is finite.
- **Paid social** exhausts the targetable audience; CTR drops, CPC rises.
- **Outbound** exhausts the addressable list at any given response rate.
- **Content / SEO** compounds, but the ramp is 12+ months from first
  publish.

The discipline: the demand-gen function's job is to be experimenting
with the *next* layer before the current one plateaus. A typical Phase
3.1 layering sequence runs paid search → paid social → content/SEO →
outbound or events, with each new layer landing 2-4 quarters before the
prior one saturates. Total lead volume keeps climbing while individual
sources hit their ceilings.

Hormozi's three phases of ad spend apply within each layer: track your
money (instrument before optimizing), lose money (the data-collection
phase that most founders quit during), print money (scale the winning
combinations). Quitting in the lose-money phase is the channel-level
analog of hitting the gas before the gates are green — same failure
mode, smaller blast radius.

## Pod replication is the unit of scale

Schatz's pod is the smallest unit of sales output that can be reliably
modeled. Once the first pod produces predictable bookings, the rest of
scaling becomes "how many pods, how fast?" Everything else — individual
rep performance, lead-supply mix, CSM ratios — is downstream from pod
math.

A pod has six locked attributes: composition (1 SDR + 1 AE + ~0.25 CSM),
ICP (same as the validated motion, not "let's also try this segment"),
quota (4-6× OTE/year), lead-supply mix (% inbound vs. outbound agreed
at pod start), coaching cadence (weekly 1:1 + monthly call review), and
reporting (pod-level KPIs visible weekly). When all six are locked, pod
variance is interpretable. When they vary, you cannot tell whether
pod 2 is underperforming because of the pod or because of the inputs.

**Pod-cloning rule:** add the next pod when capacity utilization > 70%
AND pod win rate ≥ 80% of validated baseline, both sustained ≥ 4 weeks.
Earlier is wasted hiring spend; later forfeits leads that are hitting
the funnel ceiling.

**Manager threshold:** at ~7 reps under a single manager, the org
splits. A common mistake is promoting the top AE to manage pod 2 — the
AE's coaching skills are unproven and a high-performing AE seat goes
empty. Better is to hire a sales manager externally with documented
sales-management experience and leave the top AE in their producing
seat.

## Hire 4-9 months ahead of need

Hiring lead-time is the actual rate-limiter at scale, not budget. From
job-spec to fully-ramped AE is 4-9 months in B2B: 2-3 months to source
and close the hire, 4-6 months to ramp. A bookings plan that calls for
4 net new fully-ramped reps in Q3 requires job postings open in Q4 of
the prior year.

The mirror-image failure: hiring 12 reps in one quarter to "catch up"
to the bookings plan. The manager cannot onboard 12 reps, ramp slows
to 9+ months, and the cash trough deepens for no incremental bookings.
Bursty hiring breaks both the math and the coaching system.

The Phase 3.1 hiring rhythm is steady, modeled against ramp curves, and
**started before the current plan is "missing" anything** — by the time
you can see the gap, you are already 6 months late to fix it.

## Segment ruthlessly by unit economics

The single highest-leverage move available in Phase 3.1, and the one
most often skipped: compute LTV:CAC by segment, channel, and pod, then
reallocate. HubSpot's early example — direct sales to very small
businesses showed LTV:CAC = 1.5; the same VSBs sold through value-added
resellers showed LTV:CAC = 5. They were running 12 reps on the bad
segment and 4 on the good one. Within a year of running the analysis,
the ratio was 2 on the bad segment and 25 on the good one.

This kind of move requires segment-level unit-economics reporting that
most teams do not have. Building it is a Phase 3.1 deliverable, not a
"nice to have." Without it, scaling is uniformly spread across whatever
mix accident produced — and the highest-LTV segments are systematically
under-fed.

## Quarterly Rocks™ make scaling executable

The execution cadence that converts the bookings plan into 90-day
priorities is Wickman's Rocks™ — 3-7 quarterly priorities per leader,
SMA criteria, single named owner, binary scoring. The bookings plan
sits on a slide deck until it sits on a Rock Sheet; only then is it
something a quarter can actually be measured against.

The full EOS™ install (V/TO™, Scorecard™, L10™, Accountability Chart™,
Core Processes™) belongs in 3.2. Rocks are introduced here because
scaling without 90-day execution discipline produces plans that get
abandoned by Week 6 and rationalized in the QBR.

## Cross-step KPIs

Three KPIs persist across all four Scale steps via `path_state.cross_kpis`:
**Rule of 40** (growth % + FCF margin %, target ≥ 40), **NDR** (target
> 110% sustained), and **Leadership-Health-Score** (Wickman
Organizational Checkup™, target ≥ 80, introduced as a baseline Rock in
3.1). Rule of 40 and NDR are what investors and acquirers ask about;
LH-Score predicts whether the team can execute the plan they've written.

## Exit criteria — the scale-readiness test

Step 3.1 is done when all six are true:

1. **Three gates have been green for ≥ 6 consecutive months**, not just
   the two months required to enter. Sustained, not flash readings.
2. **≥ 2 lead sources contribute > 20% each** to total qualified
   pipeline; no single-channel dependence remains.
3. **≥ 2 sales pods at quota for ≥ 2 quarters** with the six pod
   attributes locked and pod-level KPIs reviewed weekly.
4. **Segment-level LTV:CAC report exists and drives allocation
   decisions.** Quarterly reallocation between segments has happened at
   least once.
5. **Cash plan models the trough explicitly** — runway through the
   deepest projected cash point + 6 months, fundraise milestones set
   3 months before the cash floor.
6. **Rule of 40 ≥ 40 OR a credible 18-month path to 40 is on paper**
   and is the basis of the next-stage fundraise narrative.

When all six are true, the org is ready for 3.2 — the organizational
redesign that turns the founder-led scaling machine into a leadership
team's scaling machine.

## Anti-patterns

- **Hitting the gas with any gate red.** Most expensive class of
  mistake in the 11-phase roadmap. The damage is invisible for ~3
  quarters, then catastrophic.
- **Cash trough modeled too shallow.** Founders model 30% growth and
  build a runway plan; when growth comes in at 60% the trough is
  ~2× the model and runway evaporates two quarters early.
- **Hiring to plug a current bookings miss.** By the time you see the
  gap, you are 6 months from fixing it. The hiring rhythm has to lead
  the bookings plan, not chase it.
- **Single-channel scaling.** Paid search alone does not scale a B2B
  SaaS company to $50M. Layer #2 must start before #1 saturates.
- **Switching channels instead of layering.** Killing the channel that
  got you here because the new one is shinier loses you the cash flow
  that funds the experiment.
- **Skipping segment-level unit economics.** Uniform allocation across
  whatever mix accident produced is the default; ruthless reallocation
  by LTV:CAC is the move. HubSpot's 12→2 / 4→25 reallocation is the
  template.
- **No quarterly execution cadence.** A bookings plan that lives on a
  slide and not on Rocks gets blown by Week 6 of the quarter and the
  leadership team rationalizes the miss in the QBR.
- **Promoting top AE to manage pod 2.** Coaching skills are unproven,
  AE seat goes empty, both pods underperform. Hire externally.

## Connection to other steps

- **From 2.5 (GTM make-it-profitable):** The Phase 2.5 unit-economics
  gates are the same three gates that license 3.1. There is no gap
  between paths — 2.5 green directly hands off to 3.1 entry.
- **To 3.2 (Scale the org & processes):** As pods multiply and the
  founder becomes the operational bottleneck, the EOS™ install (V/TO™,
  Acc Chart™, full Rocks™ install, L10™, Scorecard™, Core Processes™)
  becomes the next forcing function. 3.1 introduces Rocks; 3.2 installs
  the rest.
- **To 3.3 (International expansion):** A clean cross-step LTV:CAC by
  segment makes the "which country first?" decision data-driven instead
  of opportunistic.
- **To 3.4 (Product-line expansion):** Healthy NDR > 110% sustained
  through 3.1 is the precondition for product-line expansion as a
  growth lever. NDR below 100% means fix retention before adding lines.
