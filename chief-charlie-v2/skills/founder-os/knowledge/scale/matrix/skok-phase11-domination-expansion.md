## Domination and Expansion — Phase 11, Matrix Partners (David Skok)

Phase 11 begins when the core ICP and core geography are showing maturity signals: pipeline is constant rather than growing, top-of-funnel cost is rising, and the strongest competitor is now operating in the same segment. The decision is what to expand into, and the temptation is to expand into everything at once.

Skok's framing: every successful scaling SaaS company eventually faces this choice. The ones that survive pick a vector, dominate it before adding a second, and protect the core while expanding. The ones that fail spread thin across all three vectors and discover that none of them produces compounding returns.

### Three valid expansion vectors

**Vertical depth** — dominate the core segment more completely. Add features the segment specifically asks for, deepen integrations with the segment's adjacent tools, raise prices for the value delivered. The defensibility move: become unrideable in the chosen vertical.

**Geographic** — replicate the proven motion in a new country or region. Often the first vector chosen because the playbook from the home market is directly portable. Risks: localization, hiring, regulatory, FX, timezone management. Wins compound when picked well — a country with similar buyer behavior and language adjacency can become a second engine in 18-24 months.

**Second product** — sell something adjacent to the current installed base. Cross-sell motion has CAC near zero (existing customer relationship), but the second product is a startup-inside-the-startup with its own PMF risk. Most second products fail to reach 20% of company ARR.

### Sequencing, not parallelism

The critical discipline: pick one vector, prove it works, then add a second. A vector "works" when it contributes ≥10% of new ARR for ≥2 consecutive quarters. Until that threshold, the second vector dilutes leadership attention and capital from the first.

Companies that pursue parallel expansion in Phase 11 (geographic + second product + vertical, all at once) almost always under-deliver on all three. The board pressure to "show ambition" makes the parallel path tempting; the math makes it expensive.

### M&A as a Phase 11 option

Mergers and acquisitions become viable in Phase 11 when the company has either (a) cash from a growth round or (b) public-company stock as currency. M&A can buy a vector — a second product, a geographic foothold, or a vertical specialization — without the build risk. But M&A integration consumes 12-18 months of leadership attention. Treated as "free growth," it almost always disappoints.

The discipline: M&A is a vector decision, not a growth-shortcut decision. Apply the same one-vector-first sequencing logic.

### Stay-in-the-cone rule

Wickman's Core Focus principle (paraphrased): expansion that strays from the company's core capabilities almost always fails. The cone of valid Phase 11 moves is narrow — products, geographies, and verticals adjacent to what the company already does well. The further from the cone, the higher the failure probability.

Test: if the new vector requires hiring an entirely new leadership team with different domain expertise, it is outside the cone.

## Key Takeaway
Phase 11 has three valid expansion vectors (vertical, geographic, second product) plus M&A as a vector-buying option. Pick ONE first; prove it contributes ≥10% of new ARR for ≥2 quarters before adding a second. Stay in the cone of core capabilities.

## When to Apply
- Core ICP pipeline growth is flattening despite Phase 9 spending
- The board is asking about international or platform expansion
- M&A bankers are reaching out with target lists
- Customer requests for adjacent products are increasing
- Company has crossed ~$50M ARR with ≥3 years of operating history
