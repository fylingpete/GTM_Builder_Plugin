## Hit the Gas — Phase 9, Matrix Partners (David Skok)

Phase 9 is where founders who built a profitable, repeatable engine in Phase 8 either compound their advantage or lose the window to a better-funded competitor. Skok's framing: until unit economics are proven, every dollar of growth spending is at risk of being wasted. Once proven, every dollar held back is wasted differently — to compounding rivals.

The phase 9 transition is binary. Either the founder commits to scaling aggressively, or they don't. There is no middle ground that doesn't either burn cash on unproven economics or surrender growth velocity to competitors.

### When the gas pedal is safe to press

Three gates, all green for at least two consecutive months:

- **LTV:CAC > 3** — every dollar acquiring a customer returns ≥$3 in lifetime gross profit
- **CAC payback < 18 months** — cash recovery window within standard SaaS norms
- **NDR > 100%** — existing customers expand faster than they churn

When all three are green, growth-stage venture capital becomes available at reasonable dilution, debt facilities become available, and reinvestment math becomes positive-sum. Hitting the gas with any of the three red is the most expensive class of mistake in the entire 11-phase roadmap.

### Cash trough — the depth surprises everyone

Skok's SaaS cash trough is the cumulative-cash chart shape that defines this phase: deeper than founders expect, and the recovery only begins after months of investment. Two reasons for the surprise:

1. **CAC is paid up-front; LTV is paid over years.** Even with healthy unit economics, every new customer makes the cash trough deeper before it makes it shallower. Faster growth = deeper trough, not shallower.
2. **Headcount ramp lead time is 4-9 months for sales reps.** Hiring an AE today produces fully-ramped quota production in 6-9 months. Bookings ramp follows hiring ramp with a lag. Founders who model "we'll hit $X bookings by Q3" without explicit ramp curves over-promise to investors and board.

### Bookings math, not revenue projections

The right scaling-stage model is `Bookings = #Reps × PPR × Capacity-Utilization`, where:

- **#Reps** is fully-ramped reps (a rep at month 3 of ramp counts as 0.4, not 1.0)
- **PPR (Productivity Per Rep)** is the annual bookings a fully-ramped rep produces — typically 4-6× their OTE
- **Capacity-Utilization** is the fraction of capacity actually filled by lead-flow

A capacity-utilization < 70% means lead supply is the bottleneck, not closing. Hiring more reps in this state is the wrong move — fix the funnel first.

### Channel layering, not channel switching

Phase 9 layers the next channels on top of the saturated Phase 4 channel — it does not switch channels. If outbound was the engine that got you here, paid ads or content go on top, not in place of. The discipline: kill criteria for each new channel are set before the test, and a layered channel that hits kill criteria is closed, not nursed.

## Key Takeaway
Hit the gas only when all three gates (LTV:CAC>3, CAC payback<18mo, NDR>100%) are green for ≥2 months. Then commit. The cash trough is deeper than your model — plan headcount lead time and bookings ramp explicitly. Layer channels; don't switch them.

## When to Apply
- All three Phase 8 unit-economics gates have been green for ≥2 consecutive months
- Founder is considering a growth round or aggressive bootstrapped reinvestment
- First scaling-stage hires (VP Sales, VP Marketing) are being planned
- Annual planning forecasts net-new ARR targets and the cash impact
