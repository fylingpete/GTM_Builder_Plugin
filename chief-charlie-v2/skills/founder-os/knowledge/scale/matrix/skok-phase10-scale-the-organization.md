## Scale the Organization — Phase 10, Matrix Partners (David Skok)

Phase 10 starts when Phase 9 has produced enough volume that the founder personally cannot continue making every decision. The symptoms are predictable: slack messages from the founder at midnight, decisions waiting on a single calendar, hiring rounds that don't fill seats, and a leadership team that defers rather than decides.

Skok's framing: the company is no longer a startup. It is a scaling organization with its own management problem. Phase 10 is the founder learning a fundamentally new job — running an organization, not running a product or running sales.

### Move from operator to leader-of-leaders

The transition is uncomfortable because the founder's prior superpower (jumping in and fixing things) becomes the company's new bottleneck. Phase 10 demands the opposite stance: stay out, set context, hire people who decide better than you in their domain, fire fast when GWC fails.

The new founder job has four levers — and only four:
1. **People** — who is in which seat
2. **Vision** — where are we going
3. **Capital** — how the cash is allocated
4. **Bar** — what "good enough" means here

Everything else delegates.

### Org structure replaces heroics

Pre-Phase-10 companies run on heroics. Whoever cares most, does it. Phase 10 replaces that with an org chart where every recurring outcome has exactly one accountable seat. The org chart is not a hierarchy diagram — it is an accountability map.

Skok's threshold: above ~50 employees, every function needs an explicit VP-level owner. Functions to expect: Sales, Marketing, Customer Success, Product, Engineering, Finance, People. Smaller companies can combine (e.g. founder is VP Product + CEO), but never combine more than two.

### Hiring velocity becomes the rate-limiter

In Phase 9, lead-supply was often the rate-limiter for growth. In Phase 10, it is hiring velocity. The math: each new pod requires 1 SDR + 1 AE + .25 CSM. Each pod takes ~4 months to hire and ~6 months to ramp. To grow bookings 2× in 12 months, founders must start the hiring engine 12 months early.

Recruiter capacity becomes a real constraint. Founder-led recruiting at scale fails — Phase 10 calls for an in-house recruiter or a high-trust external partner with a written ideal-candidate profile per role.

### Reporting cadences make the org legible

Without explicit cadences, the founder is the routing protocol — every question lands in their inbox. Phase 10 installs:

- **Weekly leadership meeting** — 60-90 minutes, scorecard review, issues identified and assigned
- **Monthly business review** — 90 minutes, P&L + KPI trends + risks
- **Quarterly planning** — half-day, set the 3-7 most important initiatives ("Rocks") for the next 90 days
- **Annual planning** — 1-2 days, refresh the long-term picture and reset the year

The cadence is the operating system. Without it, decisions queue up at the founder.

## Key Takeaway
The founder's job changes in Phase 10. Org structure with explicit accountable seats, hiring velocity as the rate-limiter, and reporting cadences as the company's nervous system — without these, growth above ~$10M ARR breaks the founder.

## When to Apply
- Decisions visibly queue at the founder's calendar
- Hiring rounds repeatedly fail to fill seats
- New customer or product issues require the founder personally to resolve
- The leadership team defers cross-functional decisions back to the founder
- Headcount has crossed ~30-50 people
