# Why Measure Product-Market Fit

Most founders talk about product-market fit as a feeling. They describe it
with lines like "you just know," or "the company starts to pull you," or
they point at rising revenue, fuller inboxes, and accelerating hiring.
Those signals are real, but they all share a fatal property for a team that
is still building in relative quiet: they only appear after fit has already
arrived. For a company in the middle of figuring out whether it is close or
far, post-hoc signals are useless as a compass. The whole point of Rahul
Vohra's PMF Engine — the methodology he assembled while building
Superhuman® — is to replace the feeling with a number, and the post-hoc
celebration with a leading indicator that can actually be moved week to
week.

## Why traditional PMF definitions fail

The standard way founders describe product-market fit is honest but
unworkable. "You know it when you see it" is a true description of the
experience and a terrible operating definition. It offers no guidance
before the moment arrives and no diagnosis when things are not moving. A
definition like that cannot:

- Be used as the target of a quarterly objective.
- Tell a team whether last quarter's work actually made the product more
  loved, or just busier.
- Distinguish a product that is genuinely close to fit from one that is
  accumulating users without affection.
- Separate the slow-burn companies from the ones that are about to stall.

The lagging signals — revenue ramps, press, hiring surges — share the
same problem. By the time any of them shows up, the decisions that produced
them are already months in the past. A founder who waits for these signals
is flying by looking in the rear-view mirror.

## What changes when you measure

Turning product-market fit into a measured quantity changes what the team
can do with it. A number can be set as an objective. A number can be
tracked across a quarter. A number can be broken down by segment to show
which part of the user base is moving and which is stuck. Most importantly,
a number can be the target of an iteration loop: form a hypothesis, ship a
change, re-measure, compare. The feel-based definition cannot support any
of this. The measured definition turns PMF from a state the founder waits
for into a lever the founder pulls on.

Concretely, once you have a number:

- OKRs become possible. "Raise the very-disappointed percentage from X to
  Y this quarter" is an objective with a real target and a real deadline.
- Progress becomes visible. A weekly check-in shows whether the number is
  trending in the right direction even before the quarterly rebuild.
- Iteration becomes scientific. Each roadmap bet has a predicted effect on
  the number, and the next measurement either confirms or challenges the
  prediction.
- Team conversations change character. Instead of arguing about whether
  the product "feels close," the team argues about which of two specific
  initiatives will move the number more.

## Why Rahul Vohra built this for Superhuman

The PMF Engine was not born in a vacuum. Rahul assembled it as a working
founder facing a specific question — is our product close or not? — and
finding every available answer inadequate. The methodology is therefore
designed for the kind of team that is in the middle of building, not for
the team that is already pulling away from the pack. Its first job is to
replace founder anxiety with a diagnostic. Its second job is to convert
that diagnostic into an optimization lever. The move from anxiety to lever
is the whole reason the engine exists.

## The key reframe — PMF as a metric, not a state

The reframe at the heart of the methodology is to stop treating product-
market fit as a binary state the company is either in or not in, and start
treating it as a continuous percentage that lives in a dashboard. A binary
state is celebratory and terminal — you either have it or you don't, and
once you have it the question stops. A continuous metric is diagnostic and
perpetual — it tells you where you are on any given week, and it keeps
telling you that even after you cross the target line. Under this reframe,
"we have PMF" is not the end of the work; it is one reading of an
instrument that will keep reading for the life of the product.

## Criteria / Rules

- PMF is treated as a quantitative percentage derived from a single
  survey question, not as a gut feeling or a set of downstream signals.
- The metric is tracked as an ongoing operating number, not as a one-time
  diagnosis.
- Founder anxiety about "do we have it yet" is replaced with a specific
  number and a plan to move that number.

## Common pitfalls

- Waiting for lagging signals — revenue, press, hiring — instead of
  instrumenting a leading indicator the team can move before those signals
  appear.
- Treating PMF as a celebratory state rather than an operating metric, so
  the measurement discipline collapses as soon as the first strong reading
  arrives.
- Confusing growth with fit. A product can be growing because of paid
  acquisition or novelty without any underlying attachment from users.
- Letting "we'll know it when we see it" absolve the team from the work of
  instrumenting the metric at all.

## When to apply

- At onboarding, when the founder is first introduced to the PMF Engine
  and needs to understand why a number is better than a feeling.
- Anytime a user reverts to qualitative language ("it feels close") and
  needs to be re-oriented to the quantitative framing.
- Whenever the team is tempted to abandon measurement after a strong
  reading, on the grounds that "we have it now."
