# The User Interview Framework (YC)

> Source: Eric Migicovsky, YC Startup School: "How to Talk to Users"

**Why this matters for PMF**: Sean Ellis surveys come AFTER the founder has at least a working hypothesis of who the engaged user is. Structured user interviews build that hypothesis from real-world signal, not founder imagination. Skip this step and the survey will tell you what you assumed, not what's true.

## The 5-Question Framework — Y Combinator

Ask these in order. They extract data about the user's life, not opinions about your idea.

1. **What is the hardest part about doing [X]?** — Confirms whether the problem you're solving is a real pain point at all.
2. **Tell me about the last time you encountered this problem.** — Forces specifics: when, where, with whom, what tools, what context. Real situations, not hypotheticals.
3. **Why was this hard?** — Different users give different reasons. The "why" feeds both your product features AND your marketing copy. Customers don't buy the "what" — they buy the "why."
4. **What, if anything, have you done to try to solve this problem?** — If they've done nothing, the problem isn't burning. Their workarounds are also your real competition.
5. **What don't you love about the solutions you've already tried?** — This is the seed of your feature set. Never ask "what features do you want?" — that's hypothetical and wrong.

## The Past-Tense Rule

People lie about future behaviour. They do not lie about past behaviour as easily because past behaviour is anchored to specifics — a date, a tool, a person, an outcome.

- "Would you use this?" → useless prediction
- "Tell me about the last time you hit this problem" → factual recall
- Always pull the conversation back to the most recent concrete instance.

## The Mom Test Cross-Reference

Rob Fitzpatrick's *The Mom Test* says the same thing in a different shape: even your mom can give you useful data if you ask about her life instead of pitching your idea. Specifics beat opinions. "I spent 4 hours on this last Tuesday" is signal. "That sounds like a great idea" is noise.

## Recognizing Signal vs Noise

**Signal**:
- Specific stories with dates, dollars, people, tools
- Existing workarounds (spreadsheets, hacks, hired help)
- Active spending or time investment in the problem
- Emotional charge when describing the pain

**Noise**:
- "That's a cool idea"
- "I might use that"
- "Lots of people probably need this"
- Compliments — they feel good and tell you nothing.

## Anti-Patterns

- **Pitching your idea.** Your job is to listen. The moment you describe your product, the interview becomes a sales call and the data dies.
- **Demoing the product.** Same problem — biases everything that follows.
- **Leading questions.** "Don't you hate when X happens?" puts the answer in their mouth.
- **Hypothetical framing.** "Would you pay $50/month?" is fiction. "What did you pay for the last solution you tried?" is fact.
- **Taking compliments at face value.** Politeness is not purchase intent.
- **Talking too much.** Aim for 70% them, 30% you. Silence pulls more out than follow-up questions.

## Operating Notes

- Keep interviews to 10–15 minutes. You don't need an hour.
- Take detailed notes — bring a co-founder or record (with permission). You won't know which facts matter until later.
- Start with 3–5 interviews, not 100. Your technique improves dramatically after the first few.
- After every batch, re-read notes and update your hypothesis of WHO the most-engaged user is. That hypothesis is what you'll later test with the Sean Ellis survey.
