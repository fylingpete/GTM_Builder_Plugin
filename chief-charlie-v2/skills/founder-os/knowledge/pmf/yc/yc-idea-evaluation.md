# Evaluating Startup Ideas (YC)

> Source: Jared Friedman, YC Startup School: "How to Get and Evaluate Startup Ideas"

**Why this matters for PMF:** Before you spend time talking to users (1.2) or building (1.3), the idea itself has to clear basic structural checks. A bad idea can't be saved by execution — and tar pits kill startups even with great founders. Sean Ellis' 40% bar is unreachable if the underlying problem isn't acute, the market isn't real, or you're not the right team to see it.

## The 10-Question Framework — Y Combinator

Don't look for a good idea in the abstract — look for a good idea for *your* team in a fertile idea space.

1. **Founder-market fit?** Most important. Are you the right team? (PlanGrid: construction expert paired with a great developer.)
2. **How big is the market?** Billion-dollar — either large now or small but growing fast (Coinbase in 2012).
3. **How acute is the problem?** If users' alternative today is *literally nothing*, that's a great problem (Brex: startups couldn't get credit cards).
4. **Do you have competition?** Counterintuitively good — competitors prove the problem is real. Just bring a new insight.
5. **Do you personally want this?** If not, worry that nobody does.
6. **Did something recently change?** New tech, regulation, or behavior creates new openings (Checkr: delivery companies suddenly needed background checks at scale).
7. **Good proxies?** A large company doing something similar in another market validates demand (Rappi = DoorDash for Latin America).
8. **Would you work on this for years?** Boring ideas grow on you — nobody starts passionate about payroll software.
9. **Is it scalable?** Software scales. Services that need rare, high-skill humans usually don't.
10. **Is this a fertile idea space?** Fintech infrastructure and vertical enterprise SaaS have astonishingly high YC hit rates. Consumer hardware and social networks: orders of magnitude lower.

## Tar Pits to Avoid

Tar pits are common ideas that *seem* easy but have hidden structural barriers. Founders walk in optimistic and stay stuck for years.

- **"App to coordinate meeting up with friends"** — has been pitched to YC for 20 years. Nobody has cracked it.
- **Generic "marketplace for X services"** without a wedge into supply or demand.
- Anything where, when you Google the idea, you find a graveyard of dead startups but no clear reason why they failed.

Diagnostic: Google the idea. Find who tried before. If you can't articulate *why they failed and why you'll succeed*, assume it's a tar pit.

## The "Why Now?" Diagnosis

Great ideas usually have a recent unlock. Ask: what changed in the last 1–3 years that makes this possible *now*, not 5 years ago?

- New technology (LLMs, mobile, cheap compute)
- New regulation (open banking, healthcare rules)
- New behavior (remote work, gig economy at scale)

If nothing has changed and the idea has been viable for a decade — assume someone competent already tried, and look for the structural reason it didn't work.

## Anti-Patterns

- **CISP — Solution in search of a problem.** "AI is cool, what can I apply it to?" Fall in love with the problem, not the technology.
- **Fearing competition.** Dropbox was the 20th cloud-storage company. Drew's insight (OS-level sync) was enough. No competition is usually a warning sign, not a green light.
- **Ignoring founder-market fit.** Picking a "huge market" you have zero context in. Resi's founders only searched inside real estate + debt financing — and found a great idea fast.
- **Hyper-optimizing for market size.** Stripe and Gusto looked boring or hard. That's *why* the opportunity was open. Hard-to-get-started and boring are features, not bugs.

## When to Apply

- You have an idea and want to stress-test it before committing 5 years.
- You're attracted to an idea because it sounds cool — not because you've validated the problem.
- You're in a space with no competitors and can't articulate why.
