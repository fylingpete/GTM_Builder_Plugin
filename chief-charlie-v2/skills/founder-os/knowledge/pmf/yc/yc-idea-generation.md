# How To Generate Startup Ideas (YC)

> Source: Jared Friedman, YC Startup School: "How to Get Startup Ideas"

**Why this matters for PMF:** 70% of top YC companies started from organic discovery — the founder's own expertise, problem, or industry insight. Founders who skip this stage and pick "interesting" ideas without authentic ownership tend to abandon them under pressure. PMF starts with picking the right problem to live with for 5+ years.

## Why Brainstorming Doesn't Work — Y Combinator

Sitting down to "think hard and come up with ideas" overwhelmingly produces tar pits — common-sounding ideas you've already heard of. The best founders don't brainstorm. They *notice* ideas through expertise, conversations, and side projects in fertile spaces.

If you must generate ideas now, use these recipes — ranked by likelihood of success.

## Recipe 1: Start With Your Team's Expertise (Best)

Ideas from this method come with automatic founder-market fit. You already see the problems insiders see, you know who to call, and you know what's been tried.

- **Resi:** founders were experts in real estate and debt financing. They constrained their search to that idea space and quickly found a great wedge.
- **PlanGrid:** construction-industry insider plus a strong developer. Outsiders couldn't even see the problem.

If your team has 3+ years of deep context in any non-trivial industry, search there *first*, not last.

## Recipe 2: Problems You've Personally Encountered

Ideally a problem you're uniquely positioned to see.

- **Vetcove:** the founders' dad was a vet ordering supplies via 1-800 numbers. Thousands of vets knew this was broken — but vets don't start tech companies. The idea sat on the table for years.

The pattern: you're at the intersection of "people who suffer from problem X" and "people who can build software." That intersection is rare and valuable.

## Recipe 7: Big Broken Industries

Any large industry that visibly *doesn't work* is probably ripe for disruption. Insurance, construction, logistics, healthcare back-office, freight, government services. Boring industries get left on the table because nobody fresh out of school dreams of them — which is exactly why the opportunity is open.

## Other Recipes (Briefly)

- **Recipe 3 — Things you wish existed.** Most intuitive, most dangerous. Likely to produce tar pits. Always ask: why doesn't this exist yet?
- **Recipe 4 — Recent changes.** New tech, regulation, or behavior. Gather Town pivoted to virtual hangouts when COVID rewired social norms.
- **Recipe 5 — Variants of successful companies.** Nuvo Cargo = Flexport for Latin America. Picked analytically: large market + good proxy + founder access.
- **Recipe 6 — Talk to people in a fertile space.** A2B's founders drove to truck stops and talked to hundreds of truckers before discovering fuel cards as the real problem.
- **Bonus — Find a co-founder who has an idea.** Solves both problems at once.

## Why "I'll Think Hard and Brainstorm" Rarely Works

You don't have the raw material. Good ideas need either deep domain context or close exposure to a real problem. Pure abstract reasoning produces ideas that *sound* novel to you but have already been tried by 50 teams who knew the space better.

The fix isn't to think harder — it's to *put yourself in idea-rich environments*: work at a startup, become an expert in something, build side projects, talk to operators in industries you respect.

## Playing the Long Game

If you don't need to start a company today:
- Become a real expert in something valuable.
- Work at a fast-growing startup to learn how things actually work.
- Build interesting side projects without forcing them to be startups (Replit started as a side project).

Future-you will thank present-you for the idea-rich position.

## When to Apply

- You want to start a company but don't have an idea yet.
- You keep brainstorming but only come up with ideas you've already seen before.
- You have deep expertise somewhere but haven't seriously looked for startup ideas inside it.
