# Finding & Talking to Users (YC)

> Source: Eric Migicovsky, YC Startup School: "How to Talk to Users"

**Why this matters for PMF:** Before you can measure PMF with the Sean Ellis 40% test, you need users who actually engage. The fastest way to get there is to manually find and talk to them — not surveys, not landing pages, not ads. A 5-minute video interview teaches you more than 5,000 form responses. The best founders keep doing this throughout the entire life of the company, because users are the only stakeholders actually paying you.

## Where to Find Users — Y Combinator

Start easy and expand outward. Don't wait for users to come to you.

- **Personal network.** Friends, acquaintances, former colleagues. Most likely to respond, sometimes less honest — but a fine starting point.
- **Co-workers and former co-workers.** Especially valuable if you're building software for an industry you used to work in.
- **LinkedIn search.** Filter by title, company size, and seniority. Send short, specific messages — not pitches.
- **Reddit, Slack, Discord communities.** Wherever the target user already congregates and complains.
- **In-person events.** Industry meetups, conferences, trade shows. Show up even without a booth — the hallway is the product.

## How to Run the Interview

- **Video, phone, or in-person only.** Never asynchronous surveys for the early conversations. You need tone, pauses, and follow-ups.
- **Build rapport first.** People are about to share things nobody has asked them about. Don't open with your idea.
- **Do NOT pitch your solution** in the first 3 interviews — ideally not at all until the very end. Mentioning your idea biases everything that follows.
- **Watch them work.** If they can screen-share their actual workflow or open the spreadsheet they hate, observe behavior — not just self-report.
- **Ask follow-ups relentlessly:** "Tell me more about that." "What do you mean by [their phrase]?" "Why was that important?"

## Past Behavior > Hypotheticals

The Mom Test rule: ask about *their life*, not your idea. People are unreliable predictors of their own future behavior, but they're reasonably accurate historians.

Good questions:
- "Tell me how you do [X] today."
- "What's the hardest part about [X]?"
- "How often do you have to do [X]?"
- "What did you do the last time this came up?"
- "What do you currently use to solve this? How much does it cost? What do you hate about it?"

These all probe what *already happened*. The answers contain real signal.

## Anti-Patterns

- **"Would you use this if it existed?"** — they'll say yes to be polite. The answer is meaningless.
- **"What features would make this better?"** — their job is problems, your job is features. Don't outsource your roadmap.
- **Yes/no questions.** You want stories, not binary answers.
- **Multiple-choice surveys.** They constrain users to *your* hypothesis. You'll never learn the thing you didn't think to ask.
- **Talking only to friends and family.** They want to be supportive, not honest.
- **Pitching in the first call.** As soon as you describe your solution, the interview becomes about your ego, not their problem.

## After the Interviews

- Organize raw notes into buckets of *problems*, not buckets of *features*.
- Identify which problem appears most often, most acutely, and is most poorly served today.
- Form an MVP hypothesis around the sharpest problem.
- Test the MVP with the same users — show them a clickable prototype and watch them use it without instructions. If they fumble, the design is wrong, not the user.

## When to Apply

- You haven't talked to a single potential user yet.
- Your roadmap decisions are based on assumptions, not interview transcripts.
- You're collecting survey data but it never tells you anything actionable.
- You're about to run the Sean Ellis 40% test and need real users who've used the product to take it.
