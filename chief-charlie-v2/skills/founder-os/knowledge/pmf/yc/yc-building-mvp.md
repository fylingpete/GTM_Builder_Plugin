# How to Build an MVP (YC)

> Source: Michael Seibel, YC Startup School: "How to Build an MVP"

**Why this matters for PMF**: The PMF survey requires actively-using customers. To get there, you need a product they can use — but the wrong MVP scope wastes months. YC's principle is simple: ship fast, solve ONE burning problem, find hair-on-fire customers who don't care about polish. Polish comes after PMF, never before.

## The Jedi vs the Smart-but-Wrong Founder — Y Combinator

Michael Seibel's midwit framing:

- **The Jedi founder** ships in 2–4 weeks, hands it to a few desperate users, iterates on what they say.
- **The "idiot" founder** also ships fast — and gets surprisingly far for the same reason.
- **The smart-but-wrong founder** runs 600 user interviews, raises a seed round, hires 100 people, builds for a year, and still hasn't shown anything to a real customer.

The right answer almost always rhymes with "build something fast, show it to users, iterate." Sophistication that delays a launch is anti-sophistication.

## Very Limited Functionality — Solve ONE Burning Problem

Your MVP should be:

1. **Fast to build** — days to weeks, not months.
2. **Very limited functionality** — solve ONE burning problem, not five mediocre ones.
3. **Aimed at a small set of desperate users** — people whose hair is on fire.

Write a 5–10 feature spec. Then cut. For each feature ask: "Does a desperate customer need this to start using the product TODAY?" If no, it's a v2 feature.

## Hair-on-Fire Customers

If your hair is on fire and someone offers you a brick, you'll buy it and hit yourself on the head with it. Your MVP is the brick. Your only job at this stage is to find customers in enough pain that they'll tolerate an ugly, half-broken solution because the alternative — keeping the pain — is worse.

You don't need 100,000 lukewarm users. You need 10–100 desperate ones. It's far better to have 100 people love your product than 100,000 who kind of like it.

## Canonical YC MVP Examples

- **Airbnb**: No payments, no map view, airbeds only, conferences only. Three guys taking photos of apartments themselves.
- **Twitch (Justin.tv)**: One page, one streamer (Justin, with a camera on his head), no video games, no chat features.
- **Stripe**: Manual overnight paperwork by the founders, almost no API surface, only worked for simple YC startups in the early days.
- **DoorDash**: Static HTML page with PDFs of restaurant menus, founder's phone number as the order line, Google Forms as the backend. No app, no driver dispatch system.

Every one of these companies looks unrecognizable from its MVP. That's the point.

## Don't Be a Fake Steve Jobs

Even the iPhone launched without an app store, without video recording, with only 2G internet. The "iPhone" people remember was the 3rd or 4th generation. If Steve Jobs needed multiple tries, you do too.

Don't fall in love with your MVP. Fall in love with your customer. The MVP is disposable. The customer relationship is not.

## Execution Tactics

- Set a hard deadline: 2–4 weeks. Treat it like a launch.
- Write a 5–10 feature spec, then cut ruthlessly.
- Recruit your first 10 customers one at a time, by hand, by name.
- Talk to them weekly. Ship a fix or feature based on what they say within 7 days.
- Track Sean Ellis ≥40% as the long-term goal — but at MVP stage, just track "do they keep using it without me reminding them?"

## Anti-Patterns

- **Waiting until polished.** "Almost ready" for 6 months means you're scared, not careful.
- **12 features instead of 1.** Each feature is a chance for the user to get confused and leave.
- **No time pressure.** Without a deadline, scope expands to fill available time.
- **Building for a broad audience.** Broad means nobody is desperate. Pick the narrowest, most-on-fire segment.
- **Hiring before launching.** A team of 10 building for an unvalidated problem is 10x the burn of a solo founder doing the same.
