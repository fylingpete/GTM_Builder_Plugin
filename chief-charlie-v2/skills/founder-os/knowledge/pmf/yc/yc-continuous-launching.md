# Continuous Launching (YC)

> Source: Kat Manalac, YC Startup School: "The Best Way to Launch Your Startup"

**Why this matters for PMF**: A "big launch day" is a myth. PMF emerges through dozens of small launches over months — each one bringing in new engaged users who can answer the Sean Ellis survey. Treat every shipped feature, every new channel, every press hit as a separate launch event. Airbnb launched 3 times before traction; Segment ran 5 launches in a single month. If launch one flops, almost nobody will remember.

## Why One Big Launch Day Rarely Works — Y Combinator

Founders prepare for months thinking they have one shot. They don't.

- Your strong convictions about the product are theoretical until users actually test it.
- Worst case: nobody notices, or people think it sucks. Neither kills your company.
- You wake up the next day and iterate. Then launch again.
- Press is nearly impossible to get at the early stage and is not a scalable growth channel anyway.

The real risk is not a bad launch — it's NOT launching, because that gives you zero data and zero new survey respondents.

## The Continuous-Launch Cadence

Weekly small launches outperform one annual hype campaign. Each launch is a small experiment:

- **New feature?** Launch it (blog post, tweet, email to existing users, post in 1 community).
- **New channel?** Launch on it (your first ProductHunt, your first ShowHN, your first Reddit post).
- **New segment?** Launch to it (cold outreach to a vertical you haven't tried).
- **New customer story?** Launch the case study.

Each event = a fresh batch of engaged users that fuels both growth and the next Sean Ellis survey.

## The One-Line Pitch Framework

Every launch needs a one-sentence description. Nail it before you launch anything.

- **Lead with what, not why.** No backstory. Just what the product does.
- **Be conversational.** Example: "PAVE lets companies plan and benchmark compensation in real time."
- **No jargon.** "Know-how and synergy platform" tells nobody anything.
- **Keep it short.** If someone can't repeat it at dinner, it's too complex.

If your one-liner is wrong, every launch on every channel will underperform.

## Where to Launch (Do All of Them, Over Time)

1. **Silent launch**: domain + name + one-line description + waitlist CTA. Every founder should have this NOW, regardless of how early.
2. **Friends & family**: test your pitch, get first feedback. Don't stay here long — they're not your real users.
3. **Strangers**: DoorDash talked to 200 small business owners before building. Get uncomfortable.
4. **Online communities**: Hacker News (ShowHN), Reddit, niche forums, Twitter/X, TikTok. Be authentic, not promotional. Robinhood got 10K signups from an "accidental" HN post.
5. **Pre-orders**: Kickstarter / Indiegogo for physical products. Don't sit on a waitlist forever.
6. **Continuous re-launches**: blog posts, HN engagement, social media, every new feature.

## Examples

- **Airbnb**: Launched 3 separate times — once with the original site, once during the DNC, once after pivoting to airbeds-and-breakfast. None of the early launches looked like a "moment."
- **Stripe**: Gradual reveal — invite-only beta with a handful of YC startups, then blog posts, then HN engagement, then API documentation as marketing. Masters of launching the same product over and over.
- **Notion**: Beta cohorts opened in waves over 2+ years. Each cohort was a launch. Each Twitter thread by an early power user was a launch.
- **Segment**: Did 5 launches in a single month — different channels, different angles, same product.

## Anti-Patterns

- **Waiting for perfection.** A launch with rough edges and 50 users beats a perfect launch that never ships.
- **Single-channel launch.** ProductHunt OR HN OR Twitter is fragile. Spread across multiple over time.
- **Ignoring small wins.** 20 new signups from a Reddit comment is a launch. Treat it like one — interview those users, ask for feedback, recruit case studies.
- **Chasing press at the early stage.** Build your own audience (email list, Twitter, community) instead. Every future launch then becomes easier and free.
- **One-shot mentality.** "We only get one chance to launch" is false and paralyzing. You get unlimited chances. Use them.

## Key Takeaway

Launch is not a one-time event — it's something you do continually, getting a little better each time, until you find the users who love you. Those users are the ones who will rate your product "very disappointed" without you on the Sean Ellis survey.
