# Do Things That Don't Scale (YC)

> Source: Paul Graham, "Do Things That Don't Scale" (essay) and Gustaf Alströmer, YC Startup School: "How to Get Your First Customers"

**Why this matters for PMF**: To survey at least 15 engaged users (the Sean Ellis directional threshold), those users have to exist first. Manual founder-led recruitment is the only reliable way to acquire the first 10 to 30 users. Scalable channels — SEO, paid ads, referrals — don't fire until much later. Founders who try to skip this stage stall before they can ever measure PMF.

## Why scalable channels fail in the first 100 users — Y Combinator

- **SEM is expensive**: keyword auctions are crowded by incumbents with bigger budgets and known LTV. You bleed cash before you understand the buyer.
- **SEO takes months to years**: ranking requires content depth, backlinks, and domain age. None of these exist for a new startup.
- **Referral programs need a base**: a referral loop requires happy users to refer. With zero users, the loop has nothing to amplify.
- **Virality is a lottery**: hoping for organic spread is not a strategy. Almost nothing goes viral; the few that do had heavy founder-led seeding underneath.

## Airbnb: the photo tour

Brian Chesky and Joe Gebbia noticed listings had bad photos. They flew to New York, knocked on hosts' doors, photographed apartments themselves, arranged the lighting, and uploaded the results. It cost money. Hosts even reused the photos on competing sites. But the founders learned hosting in the wild, and one host's coffee-chat notebook of ten years of insights became core product knowledge. No survey could have produced that.

## Brex: the YC batch

Henrique Dubugras and Pedro Franceschi launched Brex inside a YC batch. Their first customers were the other founders sitting around them — non-American founders under 25 who couldn't get a US credit card. They asked classmates one by one: "Want to try ours?" Their CAC was a hallway conversation.

## Twitch: personal calls to streamers

After five years as Justin.tv, the founders started personally calling individual gamers who streamed on the platform. Not mass emails. One call at a time. Streamers asked for higher resolution — a one-line config change. They asked for a way to make money — handwritten $20 checks went out by mail. That hand-to-hand work surfaced the insight that became Twitch.

## DoorDash: 200 small business interviews

Before writing real product code, the DoorDash founders interviewed roughly 200 small business owners in Palo Alto. They learned that delivery was the universal pain. Then they built a Squarespace landing page, took orders by phone, and drove the food themselves. The "MVP" was four founders with cars.

## Concrete tactics for your first 30 users

- LinkedIn DMs to people who match your ICP — personalized, one by one
- In-person events, meetups, conferences where your buyers congregate
- Your personal network: alumni, ex-colleagues, batch-mates, friends-of-friends
- Manual onboarding: schedule a 30-minute call, walk them through setup, watch them use it
- Concierge MVP: do the work behind the scenes by hand before automating
- Hand-deliver value: white-glove the first ten customers like they're the only ones who matter — they are

## Anti-patterns to avoid

- Waiting for SEO to "kick in" before doing manual outreach
- Hiring a marketer or growth lead before founders have closed the first ten customers themselves
- Spending on paid ads before having ten paying users
- Treating sales as beneath a technical founder
- Hoping for virality instead of manufacturing the first wave by hand
- Building features for an imagined user instead of recruiting a real one and watching them fail

The pattern across Airbnb, Brex, Twitch, and DoorDash is identical: the founders did unglamorous, unscalable, manual work to acquire their first users. That work also produced the learning that made the product worth scaling later.
