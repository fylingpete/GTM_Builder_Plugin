# Measuring Product/Market Fit (YC)

> Source: Eric Migicovsky and the Superhuman case study, YC Startup School: "How Superhuman Built an Engine to Find Product/Market Fit"

**Why this matters for PMF**: This file is the Sean Ellis 40% canon, framed by YC. It is the bridge between YC's pre-PMF advice (talk to users, do things that don't scale, care) and Founder-OS's measurement framework. Founders read this to understand WHY 40% is the threshold and HOW Superhuman operationalized it into a weekly engine. Connects directly to Founder-OS Step 1.4 (PMF-Score messen) and the segmentation / High Expectation Customer frameworks.

## The single quantitative question — Y Combinator

Send to engaged users on a regular cadence:

> "How would you feel if you could no longer use [product]?"

Three answer choices, top-box only:

1. Very disappointed
2. Somewhat disappointed
3. Not disappointed

Only "Very disappointed" counts. "Somewhat" is not partial credit.

## The 40% threshold

Sean Ellis calibrated the benchmark across roughly 100 startups. The pattern was clear: companies that crossed 40% Very Disappointed could grow with conventional channels and pricing. Companies under 40% almost always struggled to scale, regardless of how much they spent on growth. Superhuman started at 22% and used the engine to climb past 58% by systematically building for the right segment.

- Below 25%: far from PMF; major iteration needed
- 25 to 40%: getting warm; segment and double down on what works
- 40% and above: PMF achieved; growth investment now makes sense

## Why send only to engaged users

Cold leads, churned trials, and signups who never activated will skew the score downward in a way that tells you nothing about your real product.

Filter to people who:
- Used the product at least twice in the last two weeks
- Completed a meaningful action (not just signup)
- Are within their first 30 days of meaningful use (so you measure attachment, not nostalgia)

Send the survey in-app or by email. Cap the ask at one quantitative + three qualitative questions.

## Sample size bands

- Fewer than 15 responses: not enough signal, do not draw conclusions
- 15 to 39 responses: directional, useful for early iteration
- 40 or more responses: statistically meaningful, treat as a real number you can track

If you cannot get 15 engaged users to respond, the prior step is recruitment, not measurement — see "Do Things That Don't Scale."

## The four-question instrument

1. (Quant) How would you feel if you could no longer use [product]?
2. (Qual) What type of person do you think would most benefit from [product]?
3. (Qual) What is the main benefit you receive from [product]?
4. (Qual) How can we improve [product] for you?

The first question gives you the score. The next three tell you who, why, and what to fix.

## The High Expectation Customer (HXC)

Inside the Very Disappointed cohort, find the segment that:
- Loves the product the most
- Articulates the benefit most clearly
- Looks like the kind of customer you want more of

This is your High Expectation Customer. Build, market, and price for them — not for the average user, not for the loudest critic. Superhuman extracted the HXC from Very Disappointed responses, then deliberately built the next quarter of features for that segment, ignoring the Somewhat and Not cohorts. The 40% climb followed.

## How to operationalize it

- Run the survey monthly, then weekly once you have enough engaged users
- Track the percentage as a single chart; this becomes your PMF dashboard
- After every major release, watch the score move
- Read every Very Disappointed qualitative response; cluster by language
- Use the Somewhat Disappointed cohort as a roadmap: what would push them to Very?
